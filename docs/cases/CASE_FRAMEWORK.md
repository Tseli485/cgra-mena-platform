# CGRA Case Framework

## Overview

The CGRA Case Framework provides a comprehensive structure for documenting and analyzing cases involving children and guardians in vulnerable situations. This framework standardizes case documentation across all case types, domains, and age groups to ensure consistency, facilitate learning, and support tuteur (guardian/advocate) decision-making.

## Case Types

The framework recognizes 10 distinct case categories based on the primary situation or challenge:

### 1. Unaccompanied
- Children traveling or residing without parental care or guardianship
- Includes separated children, orphans, and those in migration contexts
- Focus: Legal status, family tracing, guardianship establishment, protection

### 2. Family Conflict
- Domestic disputes, parental alienation, custody disagreements
- Includes inter-generational conflicts and sibling disputes
- Focus: Mediation, legal rights, safe housing, family reconciliation

### 3. Trauma
- Cases involving abuse, violence, exploitation, or psychological harm
- Includes victims of trafficking, assault, or witnessing violence
- Focus: Psychological support, protection from perpetrators, trauma recovery

### 4. Shelter Runaway
- Children who have left home, institutional care, or shelters
- Includes street children and those in temporary shelter situations
- Focus: Safety assessment, return to care, alternative placement

### 5. Work
- Child labor, workplace exploitation, hazardous work conditions
- Includes underage employment and labor rights violations
- Focus: Labor law enforcement, education continuation, income support alternatives

### 6. Justice-Involved
- Children in contact with criminal justice system
- Includes juvenile delinquency, arrest, detention, and rehabilitation
- Focus: Legal representation, rehabilitation programs, reintegration

### 7. Health
- Medical emergencies, chronic illness, disability, mental health conditions
- Includes vaccination, nutrition, developmental delays
- Focus: Medical access, specialist treatment, disability support

### 8. Reunification
- Cases focused on bringing separated families together
- Includes post-migration family reunion and family tracing
- Focus: Documentation, travel, cross-border procedures, settlement

### 9. Readmission
- Children re-entering care, education, or social systems
- Includes return to school, re-entry after hospitalization
- Focus: Institutional coordination, catch-up support, integration

### 10. Mixed
- Cases involving multiple simultaneous challenges
- Combines elements from several case types above
- Focus: Holistic approach, multi-domain solutions, systems coordination

## Domains

The framework addresses five critical domains that intersect with case types:

### 1. Documents & Asylum
- Birth certificates, identification documents, travel papers
- Asylum applications, refugee status, legal residency
- Tasks: Document acquisition, registration, asylum procedures

### 2. Health & Medical
- Medical exams, vaccinations, chronic disease management
- Mental health support, disability assessment
- Tasks: Healthcare access, treatment coordination, health monitoring

### 3. Finances & Banking
- Income support, educational funding, disability benefits
- Bank accounts, financial literacy, economic independence
- Tasks: Benefit applications, financial planning, economic integration

### 4. Work & Employment
- Job training, employment placement, labor rights
- Skills development, workplace safety
- Tasks: Skills assessment, job matching, labor law compliance

### 5. Family & Reunion
- Family contact, tracing, reunification
- Family dynamics, relationships, communication
- Tasks: Family communication, reunion planning, relationship rebuilding

## Age Groups

Cases are differentiated by developmental stage:

### 0-2 Years
- Infants and toddlers
- Focus: Parental capacity, basic care, developmental stimulation
- Key domains: Health, family care, early intervention

### 2-6 Years
- Early childhood
- Focus: Preschool preparation, behavior, socialization
- Key domains: Health, education readiness, family stability

### 6-12 Years
- School-age children
- Focus: Education, peer relationships, skill development
- Key domains: Education, health, social integration, work prevention

### 12-18 Years
- Adolescents
- Focus: Identity, independence, preparation for adulthood
- Key domains: Education, employment, health, social-emotional development

## Case Contents

Each case document contains the following structured components:

### 1. Story
Narrative documentation of the situation:
- **Narrative**: Main account of the child/guardian situation in accessible language
- **Background**: Family history, migration journey, or historical context
- **Current Situation**: Present circumstances, living conditions, status
- **Timeline**: Key dates and events in chronological sequence

Purpose: Create human-centered understanding of the case and context for decision-making.

### 2. Problems
Systematic identification and analysis of challenges:
- **Primary Problems**: Main barriers preventing resolution (with severity rating)
- **Secondary Problems**: Contributing or downstream issues
- **Root Causes**: Underlying systemic or personal factors

Includes impact assessment for each problem.

Purpose: Ensure comprehensive problem analysis before solution design.

### 3. Legal Framework
Applicable laws and procedures:
- **International Conventions**: CRC, UNCRC, IOM protocols, regional agreements
- **National Laws**: Country-specific legislation, child protection laws, labor codes
- **Procedures**: Required administrative steps, timelines, authorities involved

Includes relevant articles/sections and application to the specific case.

Purpose: Ground solutions in legal requirements and protect rights.

### 4. Solutions
Proposed and implemented interventions:
- **Approach**: Overall strategy and philosophy guiding the case
- **Actions**: Specific steps with objectives, actors, timelines, and status
- **Outcomes**: Expected and achieved results with indicators

Includes status tracking (planned, in-progress, completed, paused).

Purpose: Document evidence-based interventions and track results.

### 5. Tuteur Obligations
Responsibilities and best practices for guardians/advocates:
- **Roles**: Primary positions the tuteur holds in relation to the case
- **Responsibilities**: Specific duties with legal basis and frequency
- **Common Mistakes**: Frequent errors or oversight patterns
- **Best Practices**: Proven strategies that work in similar situations

Highlights critical responsibilities that must not be overlooked.

Purpose: Guide tuteurs on their duties and prevent mistakes.

### 6. Lessons
Insights and learning from the case:
- **Learned**: Key lessons with context and application to similar cases
- **Challenges**: Difficulties encountered and how they were overcome
- **Best Outcome**: What approach ultimately worked

Purpose: Capture institutional knowledge and improve practice across cases.

### 7. Resources
Tools and external support:
- **Documents**: Forms, templates, guides, legal references
- **Organizations**: Relevant institutions, contacts, roles
- **References**: Academic articles, policy documents, best practice guides

Purpose: Connect cases to practical tools and expertise.

## Case Metadata

All cases include administrative tracking:
- **Created/Updated**: Timestamps for version control
- **Author**: Responsible documentarian
- **Status**: Draft, in-review, published, archived
- **Tags**: Searchable keywords
- **Related Cases**: Connections to similar or linked cases
- **Confidentiality**: Access level (public, internal, restricted)

## Usage Guidelines

### When to Create a New Case

Create a case document when:
1. A child/family situation requires sustained intervention
2. The situation involves multiple interconnected challenges
3. There is value in capturing lessons for other tuteurs
4. Legal or protection frameworks require documentation

### How to Document Cases

1. **Story First**: Begin with narrative understanding before problem analysis
2. **Comprehensive Problems**: List all problems, not just the presenting issue
3. **Legal Grounding**: Always reference applicable laws and procedures
4. **Action Tracking**: Document both planned and completed interventions
5. **Honest Reflection**: Include challenges and lessons honestly
6. **Connection**: Link to related cases and external resources

### Ongoing Case Management

Cases are living documents:
- Update regularly as situations evolve
- Track outcomes against planned interventions
- Add new lessons as case concludes
- Archive when case resolution is complete
- Maintain confidentiality throughout

## Integration with Tuteur Training

The Case Framework serves as the backbone of tuteur training:
- Cases model how tuteurs should approach similar situations
- Lessons extracted from cases inform best practice standards
- Resources in cases provide tuteurs with practical tools
- Obligations sections clarify tuteur responsibilities

## Quality Standards

Cases meeting the framework must demonstrate:
1. **Completeness**: All required sections present and substantive
2. **Accuracy**: Facts verified and sources documented
3. **Legal Compliance**: Alignment with applicable laws and conventions
4. **Practical Value**: Clear guidance for other tuteurs
5. **Child Protection**: Confidentiality maintained, dignity respected
6. **Actionability**: Recommendations are specific and achievable
