# CASE 002: Family Conflict and Child Welfare - Afghan Minor

## Story/Narrative

Zahra was 12 years old when her father brought her to a social services office in Brussels and told a caseworker he could no longer care for her. It was October 2024, and her family had arrived as Afghan refugees just three months earlier. Zahra sat beside him in silence, her school backpack at her feet, unable to understand the Flemish being spoken around her. She knew enough English from her private school days in Kabul to grasp fragments—"foster," "temporary," "assessment"—words that made her stomach clench.

Zahra's mother had died when she was nine, from an illness that should have been treatable if antibiotics had been available. Her father, Rashid, was a former university professor who had taught literature. Since his arrival in Belgium, he had been unable to find work beyond occasional day labor. His hands, once smooth from writing and reading, now had calluses from construction sites. He drank coffee continuously, spoke very little, and seemed to disappear into himself for hours.

Zahra had been raised partly by her paternal grandmother until the Taliban takeover of Kabul in 2021. For months after the fall, her father had said nothing, simply staring out windows. He had eventually arranged their passage through networks of smugglers and humanitarian workers. The journey was harrowing—Zahra remembered the cold, the hunger, the fear in the eyes of other families. She remembered her father's hands shaking as he tried to keep her warm during a night when their group hid in an abandoned building in Iran.

In Belgium, things had deteriorated further. Rashid struggled with depression and what seemed to be post-traumatic stress, though he had never been formally diagnosed. He would have good days and very bad days. On bad days, he would tell Zahra she was a burden, that he couldn't feed her properly, that she would be better off with someone else. She would reassure him—"Papa, I'm fine, I'll help"—and try to be invisible, make no demands, eat less so there would be more for him.

But there was a breaking point. One evening in late September, when Zahra asked if they could go to the grocery store because there was nothing in the kitchen, her father's response was sharper than usual. He said words in Dari that she had never heard him say before, cruel words about her being ungrateful, about her mother, about wishing things were different. Then he seemed to shrink, horrified at what he had said. He held her while he cried, apologizing over and over. The next morning, he took her to the social services office.

"I cannot be the father she deserves," he told the caseworker in halting English. "I need help."

The social worker, Martine, was experienced with families in crisis. She recognized immediately that this was not a case of parental abandonment but of a parent seeking help—something that deserved respect and protection for both Zahra and her father. She explained that there were support services available, that the family didn't have to be separated, and that temporary foster placement was not the only option. But Rashid was insistent. He felt he was failing his daughter. He needed time to stabilize.

Martine worked with a kinship network, but Zahra had no relatives in Belgium or nearby. Her extended family was scattered—an uncle in Germany (status unknown), a cousin in Pakistan (dangerous to contact). So Zahra was placed with a foster family: Anna and Michel, a Belgian couple in their 50s with teenage children of their own. They were experienced foster parents, trained in trauma-informed care. But for Zahra, it felt like another loss in a series of unbearable losses.

She was angry at her father for "rejecting" her, though part of her understood his pain. She was terrified of the foster family, despite their kindness. She had nightmares about being separated further from her father, of him moving away, of forgetting her. She refused to speak much to Anna and Michel or to do homework. She picked at food, then ate too much, then picked again. At school, she was withdrawn, though her teacher noted that she was brilliant in mathematics and seemed to have photographic memory for texts she read.

Six weeks into the placement, things shifted when Zahra's father had his own breakthrough. A case manager connected him with a psychologist who specialized in refugee trauma. The psychologist didn't push therapy immediately; instead, she helped Rashid understand that his depression and anger were normal responses to abnormal trauma. She helped him see that asking for help was not failure—it was survival. Slowly, with medication and weekly therapy, Rashid stabilized.

In parallel, Martine arranged for Zahra and Rashid to have supervised visits—first weekly, then twice weekly. These were emotional, sometimes difficult visits. Zahra would alternate between clinging to her father and pushing him away. But gradually, they began to communicate again. With Martine's help, they started talking about what had happened, about what they both needed, about their future together.

After four months of separation, Zahra moved back with her father. They had practical support in place: a social worker who visited weekly, connection to refugee support services, a youth counselor for Zahra, housing stability, and financial assistance. Rashid continued therapy. Zahra continued school. They were a family again, different from before, carrying trauma, but moving forward together.

## Tuteur Obligations (Martine's Social Worker Responsibilities)

1. **Initial Assessment and Safety Planning** - Conducted comprehensive home visit, interviewed both father and daughter separately, documented risk factors, created immediate safety plan
2. **Custody and Legal Navigation** - Coordinated with child protection court; ensured legal authority for placement; maintained ongoing custody documentation
3. **Foster Family Coordination** - Screened and matched appropriate foster family; provided orientation to Zahra's background and needs; maintained regular communication with Anna and Michel
4. **Therapeutic Liaison** - Identified trauma-specialized psychologist for father; coordinated with Zahra's school counselor; tracked progress on both mental health fronts
5. **Supervised Visitation Management** - Arranged, scheduled, and sometimes supervised father-daughter visits; tracked emotional responses; documented progress
6. **Financial Advocacy** - Assessed family's financial needs; helped secure increased refugee stipend; connected to emergency assistance programs; budgeted resources
7. **Housing Support** - Evaluated current housing; helped secure better accommodation; ensured meeting basic safety standards; conducted quality inspections
8. **Educational Coordination** - Liaison with school; ensured ESL services; kept both parents informed of academic progress; addressed behavioral concerns
9. **Reintegration Planning** - Developed comprehensive, phased plan for family reunification; identified risks; built in safeguards; scheduled gradual transition steps

## Lessons Learned

1. **Parental Mental Health Crisis is Child Welfare Crisis** - Untreated trauma in parents directly endangers children; identifying and treating parental mental health is child protection
2. **Separation as Last Resort Can Be Protective** - Temporary separation allowed both father and daughter healing time; was not punitive but preventive
3. **Family Reunification Should Be the Goal** - When parents ask for help, they often want to be better parents; support them toward that goal rather than permanent separation
4. **Supervised Visitation Bridges Separation** - Maintaining father-daughter connection during crisis prevented permanent attachment damage
5. **Cultural Community Matters** - Connecting father with other Afghan refugees reduced isolation and provided understanding that therapeutic support alone couldn't
6. **Trauma-Specialized Treatment Works** - Father's willingness to engage with a psychologist who understood refugee trauma was critical turning point
7. **Multiple Support Systems Needed** - No single intervention (therapy, housing, money, school support) alone would have succeeded; coordinated, comprehensive approach essential
8. **Hope is Powerful Medicine** - When Zahra saw her father improving and heard him speak about their future, her own healing accelerated

## Checklist for Family Conflict with Vulnerable Children

- [ ] Immediate safety assessment; determine if separation necessary to protect child
- [ ] Interview child separately from parent in neutral, safe environment
- [ ] Interview parent; assess capacity for change and willingness to engage support
- [ ] Screen for parental mental illness, substance abuse, or crisis factors
- [ ] Determine if family can remain together with intensive support services
- [ ] If separation needed, identify appropriate care (foster, kinship, residential)
- [ ] Explain process and reasoning to child in age-appropriate, native language
- [ ] Connect parent with mental health services immediately
- [ ] Establish supervised visitation schedule; prioritize maintaining relationship
- [ ] Provide foster family with detailed background information on child's needs
- [ ] Assign dedicated social worker to monitor both child and parent
- [ ] Arrange school support services; monitor academic impact
- [ ] Create comprehensive reintegration plan with clear milestones
- [ ] Schedule regular check-ins (weekly minimum) with child and foster family
- [ ] Track parent's progress in treatment/services; adjust support as needed
- [ ] Facilitate gradual increase in contact between child and parent
- [ ] Build safety protocols for reintegration (reporting, checking, support)
- [ ] Assess housing, financial, and practical needs; secure resources
- [ ] Connect family to community support (cultural, peer, recreational)
- [ ] Schedule follow-up visits for minimum 6 months post-reintegration
