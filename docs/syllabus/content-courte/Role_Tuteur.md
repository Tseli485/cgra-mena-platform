# Role of the Tuteur (Legal Guardian) for MENA

## Role Overview

A **tuteur** (legal guardian) is a court-appointed adult responsible for the legal and practical welfare of a MENA in Belgium.

### Legal Foundation
- **Appointed by family court** (tribunal de la jeunesse)
- **Based on the guardianship law** (Code Civil - Articles 389-412)
- **Acts as legal representative** in all administrative, educational, medical, and social matters
- **Full authority until age 18** (or when status changes)
- **Accountable to the court** and the child protection authorities

### Who Can Be a Tuteur?
- **Professional guardians** (trained social workers employed by NGOs or public bodies)
- **Family members** (if suitable and approved by court)
- **Volunteer guardians** (trained citizens; less common in Belgium)
- **State-appointed tuteurs** (default for unaccompanied minors)

### Scope of Responsibility
The tuteur acts as:
1. **Legal representative** – signs documents, attends hearings, manages permits
2. **Welfare advocate** – ensures safety, health, education, emotional support
3. **Decision-maker** – consents to medical treatment, school enrollment, activities
4. **Communicator** – keeps child informed, liaison with authorities and family
5. **Reporter** – submits regular updates to court and child protection services

## Daily Responsibilities

### Child Welfare & Safety
- **Ensure basic needs met**: food, clothing, shelter, hygiene
- **Monitor physical health**: observe for signs of illness, injury, neglect
- **Monitor emotional state**: watch for stress, trauma symptoms, behavioral changes
- **Respond to emergencies**: medical, behavioral, safety threats
- **Maintain safe communication**: regular check-ins, accessible to child

### Documentation & Administrative
- **Maintain records**: health documents, school reports, legal paperwork
- **Update contact information**: phone numbers, addresses, emergency contacts
- **Track deadlines**: asylum dates, school deadlines, medical appointments
- **File incident reports**: accidents, concerns, significant events
- **Keep evidence organized**: photos, medical reports, behavioral notes (if necessary)

### Education & Development
- **Facilitate school attendance**: transport, materials, uniforms
- **Support language learning**: encourage native & local language use
- **Monitor academic progress**: grades, behavior reports, attendance
- **Arrange tutoring**: if needed for language or subject support
- **Plan vocational pathway**: discuss interests, apprenticeship options

### Healthcare & Wellbeing
- **Schedule medical appointments**: GP visits, vaccinations, check-ups
- **Attend medical consultations**: understand health needs, consent to treatment
- **Arrange mental health support**: counseling, trauma-informed therapy if needed
- **Manage prescriptions**: ensure medications are taken correctly
- **Promote healthy habits**: nutrition, exercise, sleep, hygiene

## Weekly Tasks

### Check-Ins & Observation
- **At least 2 formal meetings** with the child (in person preferred)
- **Observe behavior, mood, physical state**
- **Address any immediate concerns** (conflicts, health, safety)
- **Listen to child's thoughts**, questions, concerns
- **Document observations** in case file

### Coordination with Care Facility
- **Communication with residential staff** (group home, foster family, etc.)
- **Review incident reports** from the facility (behavior, medical, behavioral)
- **Discuss dietary needs, allergies, preferences**
- **Clarify rules and expectations** with child
- **Resolve conflicts** between child and caregivers

### Educational Support
- **Meet with school contacts** (if issues arise)
- **Review grades and attendance**
- **Arrange tutoring or language support** if needed
- **Ensure uniform/materials are available**
- **Discuss extracurricular activities**

### Financial & Material Needs
- **Process pocket money allocation** (if applicable)
- **Arrange clothing, shoes, toiletries** as needed
- **Handle requests for essentials** (school supplies, medical items)
- **Budget for upcoming needs** (seasons, special events)
- **File expense reports** (if tuteur is paid position)

### Legal & Administrative
- **Follow up on pending applications** (asylum, residence permit, school)
- **Respond to communications** from authorities (CGRA, migration office, school)
- **Update personal documentation** (ID, travel documents if available)
- **Prepare for court hearings** if necessary
- **Track important deadlines**

---

## Monthly Tasks

### Formal Reporting
- **Submit monthly report to child protection authority** (timing varies by region)
  - Child's health and development
  - Educational progress
  - Family contact attempts
  - Significant incidents or changes
  - Recommendations for support
- **Document in case file** (digital or paper)
- **Keep copies** for future reference

### Financial Management
- **Review care facility invoices** (if applicable)
- **Process reimbursements** for medical, educational, or emergency expenses
- **Reconcile pocket money records**
- **File expense reports** (if required)
- **Budget for next month's needs**

### Care Plan Review
- **Schedule monthly meeting** with child and facility staff
- **Review progress on goals** (education, health, emotional wellbeing)
- **Identify any new needs** (medical, educational, social)
- **Update care plan** if circumstances change
- **Document agreements** and next steps

### Family Contact
- **Attempt contact with family** (if safe, appropriate, and possible)
- **Provide updates** to family (with child's consent)
- **Assess family reunification possibilities**
- **Document contact attempts** (successful or not)
- **Plan for visits** or communication if appropriate

### Welfare Assessment
- **Evaluate child's overall wellbeing**
- **Consider any service gaps** (medical, educational, psychological)
- **Identify upcoming transitions** (age milestones, status changes, school progression)
- **Meet with child privately** (away from facility staff)
- **Address any complaints or concerns** the child raises

## Common Challenges & Solutions

### Challenge: Child Refuses School
**Signs**: Avoids classes, claims illness, runs away on school days, poor grades
**Root causes**: Language barrier, peer conflict, trauma-related anxiety, cultural adjustment
**Solutions**:
- Meet with school + child to identify specific issue
- Arrange language support or tutoring
- Explore peer mentoring or buddy system
- Consider school counselor / psychologist intervention
- Temporarily adjust schedule if needed (part-time initially)
- Address underlying trauma through mental health support
- Celebrate small achievements to rebuild confidence

### Challenge: Health Issues (Undisclosed or Delayed)
**Signs**: Behavioral changes, physical symptoms appearing late, trust issues around healthcare
**Root causes**: Previous medical trauma, fear of authorities, untreated conditions
**Solutions**:
- Build trust before rushing to appointments
- Bring interpreter if needed; ensure child understands medical process
- Schedule mental health support alongside physical care
- Explain medical confidentiality clearly
- Allow child to ask questions; slow down conversations
- Request culturally competent healthcare provider if possible
- Document all health interactions

### Challenge: Family Contact Is Complicated
**Signs**: Child wants contact but family is unsafe; child refuses contact; family's whereabouts unknown
**Root causes**: Trafficking history, abusive family, family denies child fled, family in danger zone
**Solutions**:
- Conduct **safety assessment** before attempting contact
- Consult with trauma-informed professionals
- Use Red Cross family-tracing services if address unknown
- Allow supervised communication initially
- Never force contact; respect child's autonomy
- Document safety concerns and keep records confidential
- Plan reunification only if safe and in child's best interest

### Challenge: Behavioral Issues & Conflict with Staff
**Signs**: Rule-breaking, aggression, running away, tension with caregivers
**Root causes**: Trauma activation, cultural misunderstanding, grief, testing boundaries
**Solutions**:
- Separate child from situation to de-escalate
- Listen to child's perspective without judgment
- Investigate underlying cause (not just the behavior)
- Involve child in problem-solving ("What would help?")
- Set clear, fair, explained consequences
- Arrange trauma-informed behavioral support
- Mediate between child and facility staff
- Consider medication review if extreme behavior

### Challenge: Asylum Process Delays or Rejections
**Signs**: Appointment cancellations, decisions delayed, protection status denied
**Root causes**: Backlogs, documentation gaps, credibility issues, case complexity
**Solutions**:
- Contact CGRA directly to check case status
- Arrange legal representation if rejection likely
- Gather supporting evidence (medical reports, school records, witness statements)
- Prepare child for interview (without coaching)
- Appeal decisions using legal aid services
- Connect with advocacy organizations (Caritas, lawyer)
- Keep child informed of process; manage expectations

### Challenge: Tuteur-Child Mismatch or Trust Issues
**Signs**: Child avoids tuteur, refuses to share information, verbal conflict
**Root causes**: Cultural differences, language barriers, personality clash, trauma-based distrust
**Solutions**:
- Facilitate mediation session
- Invite child to express concerns (confidentially if needed)
- Consider whether reassignment is necessary
- In interim, increase facility staff involvement
- Slow down relationship-building; no forced bonding
- Be consistent and transparent in all interactions
- Acknowledge cultural differences; adapt communication style
- Seek supervisor support for complex dynamics

### Challenge: Age Assessment Disputes
**Signs**: Identity doubted, radiological age testing ordered, child distressed by process
**Root causes**: Missing documents, appearance not matching claimed age
**Solutions**:
- Request medical opinion (not just radiological; consider maturity, development)
- Gather alternative evidence (testimonies, school records, cultural context)
- Provide psychological support during assessment process
- Appeal if result seems inaccurate
- Ensure legal representation during hearings
- Document all evidence thoroughly
- Know that age uncertainty ≠ assumption of adulthood

## Quick Reference: Tuteur Preparation Checklist

**Before first meeting with child:**
- [ ] Read case file completely
- [ ] Understand nationality, background, family situation
- [ ] Identify language(s) spoken; arrange interpreter if needed
- [ ] Know legal status and asylum timeline
- [ ] Familiarize with care facility staff and location
- [ ] Review immediate health/safety concerns
- [ ] Prepare introduction in child's language (key phrases)

**First week tasks:**
- [ ] Formal introduction meeting with child
- [ ] Tour of care facility and immediate surroundings
- [ ] Explain tuteur role in simple, clear language
- [ ] Assess immediate needs (medical, material, emotional)
- [ ] Establish communication preferences (phone, in-person, frequency)
- [ ] Register with school (if not done)
- [ ] Schedule first GP appointment
- [ ] Set up case file system
- [ ] Attend first court hearing (if scheduled)

**Ongoing (repeat monthly):**
- [ ] In-person meetings with child (minimum 2/week recommended)
- [ ] Facility coordination meetings
- [ ] Family contact attempts
- [ ] Health and education monitoring
- [ ] Monthly report to authorities
- [ ] Care plan review and updates
