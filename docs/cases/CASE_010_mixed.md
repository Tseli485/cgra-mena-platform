# CASE 010: Mixed Vulnerabilities 1 - Trauma, Family Conflict & Health Crisis

**Child Profile:**
- Name: Amara K.
- Nationality: Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC)
- Age: 11 years old
- Gender: Female
- Location: Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of Congo
- Case Status: Active intervention (8 months)
- Tuteur: Mme. Justine Mukanda (social worker)
- Date Case Opened: January 15, 2026

---

## Story and Narrative

Amara came to the attention of child protection services in January 2026 after a teacher at her school noticed she had become withdrawn and was frequently absent. The girl lived with her mother, Marie, in a densely populated neighborhood of Kinshasa. Her father had been killed during community violence in 2023 when Amara was only nine years old. Marie worked irregular jobs as a street vendor, earning barely enough to feed her three children. The household consisted of Amara, her younger brother Darius (age 8), and her infant sister Zola (age 2).

The combination of unresolved grief from her father's death, deteriorating family relationships, and a severe health crisis created a perfect storm for Amara's wellbeing. In late 2025, tension had escalated between Marie and her extended family over inheritance disputes related to Amara's late father's property. The grandmother, who had been helping support the family, withdrew her assistance in anger, deepening the family conflict. Without her grandmother's contribution, the family's precarious economic situation became critical.

Around the same time, Amara developed a persistent cough and fever. Her mother, overwhelmed and lacking funds for medical care, delayed taking her to a clinic. By the time Amara was examined in December 2025, she had developed tuberculosis (TB). The diagnosis was devastating: the treatment regime would require 6-8 months of intensive medication, regular clinic visits, and nutritional support—all resources the family simply did not have.

The convergence of three major stressors—unresolved trauma from her father's death, escalating family conflict that isolated the household from their support network, and a serious chronic illness—created a cascading crisis. Amara's school attendance dropped from 85% to 40% within two months. She stopped playing with other children. She began having nightmares and outbursts of anger. Her mother, stretched to the breaking point, occasionally struck Amara out of frustration.

When the school's social worker filed the report, Mme. Justine Mukanda was assigned as Amara's tuteur. The case required simultaneous intervention across multiple domains: trauma counseling, family mediation, medical case management, economic support, and educational re-engagement. Over eight months, a coordinated approach addressed each vulnerability while recognizing how they were interconnected.

## Problems Identified

1. **Unresolved Trauma from Paternal Death**
   - Witnessed or experienced violence-related loss
   - Delayed grief responses manifesting as anger and withdrawal
   - No formal bereavement support received in 2-year period

2. **Family Conflict and Breakdown of Extended Support Network**
   - Property dispute between maternal family members
   - Grandmother's withdrawal of critical financial and emotional support
   - Household isolation from extended family safety net
   - Increased stress on primary caregiver (mother) with no support outlet

3. **Chronic Health Crisis (Tuberculosis)**
   - Infectious disease requiring 6-8 months of treatment
   - Delayed diagnosis due to economic barriers
   - No access to consistent medical supervision
   - Nutritional deficiency compounding immune response

4. **Economic Vulnerability and Poverty**
   - Household income insufficient even before health crisis
   - Inability to afford medical care, treatment medications
   - Loss of grandmother's contribution deepened poverty
   - No stable employment for primary caregiver

5. **Educational Disruption and School Disengagement**
   - From 85% to 40% attendance in two months
   - Risk of grade repetition or dropout
   - Social isolation from peer relationships at school
   - Missed learning accumulating; academic gap widening

6. **Psychological Trauma and Behavioral Changes**
   - Nightmares and sleep disturbances
   - Anger outbursts and emotional dysregulation
   - Social withdrawal and reduced peer interaction
   - Early signs of depression

7. **Inadequate Parental Capacity and Caregiver Burnout**
   - Mother overwhelmed by competing crises
   - Insufficient emotional resources for children's psychological needs
   - Incident of physical punishment (hitting) as stress response
   - No respite care or parental support system in place

8. **Vulnerability of Younger Siblings**
   - Brother (age 8) showing behavioral changes related to family stress
   - Infant sister (age 2) at risk due to family instability
   - No separate assessment/monitoring of younger children initially

9. **Lack of Coordinated Care and Service Navigation**
   - No integrated approach across medical, social, psychological domains
   - Family unaware of available assistance programs
   - Healthcare providers unaware of family's broader vulnerabilities
   - No case management to coordinate services

10. **Risk of Child Labor or Exploitation**
    - Economic desperation increasing risk
    - Lack of supervision during school absences
    - Vulnerability to street recruitment or informal work

## Solutions Implemented

1. **Immediate Medical Intervention and TB Case Management**
   - Enrollment in DRC national TB program (free treatment)
   - Supervised treatment regimen through registered health facility
   - Monthly follow-up consultations; sputum monitoring at 2, 5, and 8 months
   - Connection to directly-observed therapy (DOT) program for medication adherence

2. **Nutritional Support Program**
   - Monthly supplementary food package (flour, beans, oil, fortified mix)
   - Nutritional counseling for mother regarding TB patients' needs
   - Coordination with health facility to monitor nutritional status
   - Cost: ~$30/month from child protection program budget

3. **Psychosocial Support and Trauma Counseling**
   - 16 individual counseling sessions with child psychologist (baseline trauma assessment, grief processing, coping skills)
   - Group play therapy with other children experiencing loss (monthly)
   - Psychoeducation for mother regarding trauma responses and behavioral expectations
   - Crisis support line number provided; mother trained in de-escalation techniques

4. **Family Mediation and Conflict Resolution**
   - Three-session mediation with extended family members facilitated by trained mediator
   - Property dispute negotiated; settlement reached allowing grandmother's re-engagement
   - Family meeting to discuss mutual support needs and realistic contributions
   - Written agreement documenting grandmother's resumption of ~$10/month support

5. **Economic Support and Emergency Assistance Grant**
   - Assessment completed documenting household income below poverty threshold
   - Family linked to government emergency assistance program
   - Grant application submitted (supporting documentation: medical diagnosis, income verification, school records)
   - $200 emergency grant approved for 6 months; prioritized for medical, nutrition, and school supplies

6. **Educational Re-engagement and Academic Support**
   - School re-engagement plan developed with teacher; reduced initial hours (mornings only, 3 weeks)
   - After-school tutoring program (2 hours/week) to catch up on missed material
   - School social worker assigned as liaison to tuteur; weekly check-ins
   - Arrangement for flexible schedule accommodation during intensive TB treatment phase

7. **Strengthened Parental Capacity and Family Support**
   - Mother enrolled in 4-week parenting support group (stress management, child development, positive discipline)
   - Individual counseling for mother (5 sessions) addressing grief over partner's death and current stress
   - Respite care arrangement: Amara spends one afternoon weekly with trained childcare worker, providing mother with break and stress relief
   - Information and resources on available services, child behavior, and health literacy

8. **Monitoring and Accountability System**
   - Bi-weekly tuteur visits (initially); then monthly once stability achieved
   - Multi-disciplinary case conference at baseline, 3 months, and 6 months
   - Safety and wellbeing indicators tracked: school attendance, behavioral changes, medical adherence, family interaction patterns
   - Formal case review at 6 months to determine next phase

## Lessons Learned

1. **Multiple Vulnerabilities Interact and Compound**
   - Amara's case demonstrates how trauma, family conflict, health crisis, and poverty are not separate problems but deeply interconnected. The father's death created unresolved grief that made Amara vulnerable to family stress; the family conflict removed the grandmother's support exactly when the health crisis required maximum resources; the economic desperation made medical care inaccessible, worsening health; and poor health prevented school attendance, creating educational vulnerability and further isolation. Interventions must address the full system, not just isolated issues.

2. **Timing of Crisis Intervention is Critical**
   - Had intervention occurred in 2024 (during the early grief responses or when family conflict began), many of the severe consequences might have been prevented. By the time the case was opened in January 2026, Amara had suffered nearly 18 months of untreated trauma, months of untreated TB, and months of missed education. Earlier identification of risk factors—behavioral changes at school, family relationship breakdown—could have enabled prevention-focused intervention.

3. **Schools are Critical Early Warning Systems**
   - The school social worker noticed Amara's withdrawal before the family sought help. Schools are often the first setting to observe behavioral changes, absences, and signs of distress. Investment in school-based social workers, teacher training on child vulnerability indicators, and formalized reporting procedures can enable earlier intervention.

4. **Caregiver Stress is a Protective Factor Risk**
   - Amara's mother's burnout led to physical punishment and delayed medical care. While Marie was not inherently an abusive parent, the convergence of stressors pushed her beyond her capacity. Supporting caregiver wellbeing—through respite care, counseling, parenting education, and economic support—is essential for protecting children in vulnerable households.

5. **Family Conflict Resolution can Restore Critical Support**
   - The property dispute with the grandmother seemed like a legal matter but had direct protective consequences. When mediation allowed the family conflict to be addressed and the grandmother to re-engage, financial support resumed and Amara gained an additional protective adult. Family-focused interventions can restore informal support networks that are often more sustainable than external services.

6. **Coordinated, Multi-Sectoral Approach is Non-Negotiable**
   - The case required simultaneous action across health, education, psychology, social protection, and legal domains. Any sector working in isolation would have failed. The tuteur's role as coordinator—ensuring TB treatment, school enrollment, counseling, family mediation, and economic support all proceed in parallel with attention to their interaction—was essential. Case management capacity for complex cases is not a luxury but a necessity.

7. **Economic Support Must be Paired with Other Interventions**
   - Money alone would not have solved Amara's problems, but without economic support, all other interventions were compromised. The emergency grant that enabled school enrollment while receiving TB treatment, combined with nutritional support and family mediation, created the foundation for psychological and educational recovery. Economic vulnerability must be addressed as part of a comprehensive approach.

8. **Formal Health Systems and Social Protection Systems Must Be Integrated**
   - The TB program had excellent medical protocols but no awareness of the family's broader vulnerabilities or economic barriers to adherence. The social protection system could provide financial support but didn't know about the ongoing medical crisis. Linkages between health services, education, and social protection systems ensure comprehensive support and prevent gaps.

9. **Tailored Support for Different Child Developmental Stages**
   - Amara (age 11) needed trauma counseling and skill-building for emotional regulation. Her brother (age 8) needed different support; the assessment revealed he was showing behavioral changes but was less severe than Amara's. The infant (age 2) needed developmental monitoring and safe caregiving. The same case requires different interventions for each child based on developmental stage and specific vulnerabilities.

10. **Sustainability Requires Building Family and Community Capacity**
    - External services like counseling, tutoring, and grants are necessary but must be paired with building the family's own capacity. The parenting education for mother, the restored relationship with grandmother, and the school re-engagement plan all work because they build the family's own resilience and resources. Interventions that create dependency are ultimately unsustainable.

## Checklist for Mixed Vulnerability Cases (Trauma + Family Conflict + Health Crisis)

### Initial Assessment (Week 1-2)
- [ ] Comprehensive safety assessment; identify immediate risks
- [ ] Child's understanding of situation and voice regarding needs/preferences
- [ ] Health assessment: current medical status, treatment needs, medication history
- [ ] Psychological assessment: trauma symptoms, behavioral changes, emotional state
- [ ] Family composition and relationships; identify protective adults
- [ ] Family history of trauma, loss, or violence affecting current situation
- [ ] Educational status: school enrollment, attendance, academic performance
- [ ] Economic situation: household income, expenses, available resources
- [ ] Extended family relationships and informal support network
- [ ] Previous involvement with child protection or health services
- [ ] Cultural factors affecting family dynamics, healing, and preferences

### Medical Coordination (Ongoing)
- [ ] Medical diagnosis confirmed and documented
- [ ] Treatment plan established with clear medication/service schedule
- [ ] Healthcare provider identified as primary contact and responsible party
- [ ] Regular clinic appointments scheduled; transportation arranged if needed
- [ ] Medication dispensing system established (pharmacy, dosing schedule, storage)
- [ ] Nutritional assessment and supplementation plan developed
- [ ] Adherence monitoring system in place (clinic visits, sputum tests, weight monitoring)
- [ ] Side effects assessment and management plan
- [ ] TB status tracked: diagnostic phase, treatment phase, recovery phase
- [ ] Coordination between TB program and child protection services

### Psychological and Trauma Support (Baseline)
- [ ] Trauma screening completed; severity of symptoms assessed
- [ ] Child's grief process evaluated; unresolved losses identified
- [ ] Behavioral changes documented; functional impact assessed
- [ ] Sleep disturbances, nightmares, anxiety tracked
- [ ] Suicide/self-harm risk assessed
- [ ] Child referred to qualified counselor or trauma specialist
- [ ] Counseling modality selected based on age and needs (play therapy, trauma-focused CBT, narrative therapy)
- [ ] Mother/caregiver psychoeducation: understanding trauma responses, realistic expectations
- [ ] Crisis support numbers and protocols established
- [ ] Coordination with health providers regarding psychosomatic effects

### Family Conflict Resolution (Early Phase)
- [ ] Dispute or conflict clearly documented and history traced
- [ ] Stakeholders identified (immediate family, extended family, community leaders)
- [ ] Legal issues (property, inheritance) separated from emotional conflict
- [ ] Mediator or conflict resolution specialist identified and engaged
- [ ] Mediation sessions scheduled with all key parties
- [ ] Child's safety prioritized throughout mediation process
- [ ] Interim agreements addressing immediate needs while dispute is resolved
- [ ] Documentation of any agreement reached; follow-up accountability

### Caregiver Support and Capacity Building (Primary Focus)
- [ ] Caregiver's own trauma, stress, and mental health assessed
- [ ] Parenting education program offered (stress management, child development, discipline)
- [ ] Individual counseling for caregiver addressing grief/loss
- [ ] Information provided on child protection resources, rights, services
- [ ] Respite care or break time arranged to reduce burnout
- [ ] Economic information provided; applications supported
- [ ] Recognition of caregiver's strengths; progress acknowledged
- [ ] Connection to caregiver support groups or networks
- [ ] Monitoring of caregiver's capacity; early intervention if deteriorating

### Educational Support (Re-engagement)

- [ ] Teacher and school social worker identified as communication partners
- [ ] Child's educational needs assessed (grade level, gaps, learning challenges)
- [ ] Flexible return-to-school plan developed (phased entry, accommodations)
- [ ] Tutoring or academic catch-up support arranged
- [ ] School notified of medical treatment schedule and accommodations needed
- [ ] Attendance monitoring system established; barriers to attendance addressed
- [ ] School counselor or social worker assigned for ongoing support
- [ ] Peer reintegration supported (group activities, buddy systems)
- [ ] Regular progress updates to tuteur; school-home communication

### Economic Support (Access and Sustainability)
- [ ] Household economic situation comprehensively assessed
- [ ] Income sources and stability documented
- [ ] Major expenses identified and prioritized
- [ ] Available assistance programs identified (government, NGO, community)
- [ ] Applications completed with supporting documentation

- [ ] Grant/assistance disbursement monitored and tracked
- [ ] Funds allocated to priorities: medical, nutrition, education, housing
- [ ] Exploration of additional income-generating opportunities for household
- [ ] Economic plan developed for sustainability beyond emergency assistance

### Safety Monitoring (Ongoing)
- [ ] Danger/risk indicators identified and tracked
- [ ] Home visits conducted at regular intervals (bi-weekly initially, monthly after stabilization)

- [ ] Child's wellbeing indicators tracked: physical health, behavioral changes, emotional state
