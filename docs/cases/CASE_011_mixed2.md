**Child Profile:**
- Name: Oumou D.
- Nationality: Republic of Guinea
- Age: 7 years old
- Gender: Female
- Location: Conakry, Republic of Guinea
- Case Status: Active intervention (10 months)
- Tuteur: Monsieur Amadou Diallo (child protection officer)
- Date Case Opened: August 20, 2025

## Story and Narrative

Oumou was recovered by police and child protection workers in August 2025 after being trafficked for domestic servitude. She was found in a private residence in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of Congo—having been moved across borders through a trafficking network that operated across West and Central Africa. At the time of recovery, Oumou was 7 years old, severely malnourished, exhibiting acute trauma responses, and had never attended school despite her age.

Oumou's story began in poverty. She was born in Conakry, Guinea, to a single mother, Aïssatou, who worked as a street cleaner earning barely enough to feed herself. Oumou never knew her father. At age 3, Oumou's mother developed severe mental illness—likely schizophrenia with significant functional impairment. For years, Aïssatou worked intermittently but increasingly struggled to care for Oumou. Relatives took the child in periodically, but the extended family was also poor and unable to absorb the additional burden.

When Oumou was 5 years old, a woman named Mama Awa approached Aïssatou at the market. Mama Awa presented herself as a successful businesswoman who could provide opportunities for girls. She promised that Oumou would learn domestic skills, attend school, and send money back to her mother. The promised arrangement was never formalized—no contract, no documentation, no family agreement. Aïssatou, desperate and overwhelmed, accepted. She believed her daughter would be better cared for than she could manage.

Instead, Oumou was taken to Mama Awa's compound in Kinshasa, where she was confined, forced to work 14-16 hours per day in domestic labor (cooking, cleaning, childcare), severely beaten for minor infractions, and denied schooling entirely. The "learning domestic skills" became exploitation; the promise of school never materialized. Oumou was one of six girls in the household, ranging from age 5 to 15, all trafficked under similar circumstances. She was isolated from communication with her mother.

After two years of trafficking, in August 2025, a neighbor reported suspicious activity to police. During a police raid, Oumou and the other girls were recovered. Oumou was severely traumatized, malnourished (height and weight both below 3rd percentile for age), with unexplained injuries, and displaying acute psychological distress. She had never been to school; her developmental level was significantly delayed. She could not read, count, or perform basic self-care tasks. She exhibited selective mutism in response to trauma, communicating rarely and only in whispers.

Oumou was repatriated to Guinea through an international trafficking victim support process. Upon arrival, she was placed in a rehabilitation center for trafficking survivors. Monsieur Amadou Diallo was assigned as her tuteur to coordinate her recovery across the following months.

The convergence of three major vulnerabilities—exploitation and trafficking trauma, severe psychological trauma responses, and profound educational delay from lost schooling during critical developmental years (age 5-7)—created extraordinary challenges. Oumou required simultaneous intervention in trauma recovery, physical and mental health rehabilitation, family reunification assessment, educational assessment and acceleration, and protection from re-trafficking.

## Problems Identified

1. **Human Trafficking and Exploitation**
   - Transnational trafficking across borders (Guinea to DRC)
   - Forced labor in domestic servitude; confinement and isolation
   - Physical abuse; exploitation without compensation or documentation
   - Trafficking facilitated by family poverty and caregiver desperation
   - No legal protections; informal arrangement without documentation

2. **Complex Psychological Trauma**
   - Acute PTSD from trafficking, confinement, and physical abuse
   - Severe fear responses; hypervigilance; difficulty trusting adults
   - Selective mutism and communication difficulties
   - Dissociation and emotional numbing as trauma responses
   - Developmental trauma affecting attachment and relationship capacity

3. **Severe Malnutrition and Physical Health Crisis**
   - Gross malnutrition (height and weight both below 3rd percentile)
   - Dehydration; vitamin and mineral deficiencies
   - Untreated infections and injuries; scarring from beatings
   - Delayed physical development; weakness and fatigue
   - Immunosuppression from malnutrition; frequent infections

4. **Profound Educational Delay and Developmental Lag**
   - Age 7 with no school attendance (missed ages 5-7, critical language/literacy period)
   - Non-verbal or near-non-verbal; severe language delay
   - Pre-literacy and pre-numeracy skills not developed
   - Cognitive development affected by malnutrition and trauma
   - Motor skill delays; self-care tasks require instruction

5. **Maternal Mental Illness and Family Dysfunction**
   - Primary caregiver (mother) with severe untreated mental illness (likely schizophrenia)
   - Mother's work history unstable; income insufficient
   - Caregiver incapacity led to child trafficking by default
   - Mother in crisis state at time of repatriation; hospitalized
   - Limited capacity for immediate childcare upon reunification

6. **Family Separation and Severed Relationships**
   - Oumou separated from mother for 2 years; believed abandoned
   - No communication during trafficking period
   - Mother unaware of true situation; believed false trafficking narrative
   - Uncertainty about father; no paternal involvement
   - Extended family capacity limited; housing and economic constraints

7. **Poverty and Economic Vulnerability**
   - Underlying poverty that facilitated trafficking
   - Mother's irregular income insufficient for family needs
   - No stable housing at time of repatriation
   - Cost of Oumou's education and rehabilitation beyond family resources
   - Risk of re-trafficking if family poverty not addressed

8. **Re-trafficking Risk and Vulnerability**
   - History of trafficking creates known risk; traffickers may target again
   - Community awareness of trafficking event; potential stigma
   - Poverty creates ongoing vulnerability to trafficking approaches
   - Isolation and emotional needs create susceptibility to manipulation
   - Inadequate protective systems if family cannot provide safety

9. **Lack of Legal Documentation and Identification**
   - Oumou's birth registration status unclear
   - No documentation of trafficking crime or perpetrators
   - Legal status of mother's guardianship uncertain
   - No formal evidence for prosecution of traffickers
   - Difficulty accessing services without proper identification

10. **Institutional and Community Gaps**
    - Limited rehabilitation centers for trafficking survivors in Guinea
    - Few therapists trained in trauma and trafficking-related issues
    - School system lacks capacity for catch-up education and trauma-informed pedagogy
    - Limited coordination between trafficking response, health, education, and family services
    - Community awareness and prevention efforts inadequate

   - Wound care and infection treatment; antibiotic therapy as needed
   - Vaccination and immunization assessment; completion of routine vaccines
   - Nutritional rehabilitation program: specialized food, micronutrient supplementation
   - Weekly monitoring of weight, height, vital signs; health tracking

2. **Psychological Trauma Support and Recovery**
   - Initial psychosocial first aid; establishment of safety and basic trust
   - Referral to trauma-informed child psychologist specializing in trafficking survivors
   - Play-based therapy; trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT) adapted for age
   - Techniques for managing dissociation, hypervigilance, fear responses

   - Sessions with therapist twice weekly (intensive); ongoing monitoring

## Lessons Learned

1. **Trafficking is a Symptom of Poverty and System Failure, Not Primarily a Criminal Issue**
   - Oumou was trafficked because her mother was desperate and had nowhere else to turn. The criminal act of trafficking was enabled by systemic failures: lack of childcare, inadequate services for mothers with mental illness, poverty without social safety net, and lack of formal education opportunities. Prevention requires addressing the vulnerability factors that make children and families susceptible. A purely law-enforcement approach that doesn't address poverty, disability, and family support gaps will fail to prevent trafficking.

2. **Families in Extreme Poverty May Participate in Trafficking Unknowingly or Desperately**
   - Aïssatou agreed to a "domestic service and education arrangement" not fully understanding it was trafficking. Her mental illness impaired her judgment; her poverty made any arrangement seem better than her current capacity. Tuteurs must approach families with compassion, not judgment. Reintegration requires supporting the family member who facilitated trafficking to understand what happened and prevent it from happening again, not punishment or further isolation.

3. **Trafficking Survivors Require Intensive, Specialized, Trauma-Informed Care**
   - Oumou's selective mutism, severe trauma responses, and developmental delays required specialized psychological intervention, not standard counseling. The integration of play-based therapy, trauma-focused CBT, careful pacing of relationships, and recognition of dissociation as a trauma response was essential. Mainstream services—schools, health facilities, child protection systems—must develop trauma-informed capacity or trafficking survivors will fall through the cracks.

4. **Educational Delay from Missed Schooling is a Lifelong Vulnerability**
   - Oumou missed the critical ages 5-7 during which language literacy foundations are established. Even with intensive catch-up education, she will likely always be somewhat behind her age cohort. The earlier trafficking is discovered and the child is removed, the better. The case also illustrates that educational recovery requires recognition of developmental trauma, not standard grade-level instruction; Oumou needed language development support, not standard Grade 1 curriculum.

5. **Maternal Mental Illness Must Be Treated Alongside Family Reunification**
   - Returning Oumou to an untreated mother with schizophrenia would have failed both the child and the mother. The mother's treatment—psychiatric medication, psychoeducation, parenting support, and economic stabilization—was as essential as Oumou's trauma recovery. Family reunification is not just about the child; it requires ensuring the caregiver has the capacity, resources, and support to provide safe care.

6. **Poverty Reduction is Re-trafficking Prevention**
   - Oumou remains at risk for re-trafficking as long as her mother lives in poverty and housing instability. The economic support—government assistance, housing support, vocational training exploration—is not just about comfort; it directly reduces the vulnerability factors that make a child susceptible to trafficking. Re-trafficking prevention requires sustained economic support, not just crisis intervention.

7. **Community Knowledge of Trafficking Event Can Create Stigma or Risk**
   - Once a child is known in the community as a "trafficking survivor," she is simultaneously at higher risk for re-trafficking (traffickers may target her specifically) and at risk for social stigma and exclusion. The community awareness work with Oumou's neighborhood required careful balance: enough awareness for protective vigilance, but not enough to stigmatize the child or make her an object of pity or gossip.

8. **Re-trafficking Risk Assessment Must Be Ongoing, Not One-Time**
   - Oumou's risk was highest at reunification when she transitioned to living with her mother in the community. The monitoring and protective planning must continue beyond case closure. The risk may actually increase at adolescence if she encounters trafficking recruiters again. Regular, ongoing assessment and protective relationship maintenance is needed.

9. **Legal Prosecution of Traffickers Can Conflict with Survivor Protection**
   - If the trafficking perpetrator is prosecuted, the survivor may need to testify or provide evidence. This can re-traumatize the child and expose her to the perpetrator. The tuteur must balance supporting legal accountability with protecting the survivor from re-traumatization. Sometimes, immediate survivor care and protection takes priority over prosecution; the legal process can follow once the child has recovered.

10. **Trafficking Survivors Require Longer-Term Case Management Than Other Vulnerable Children**
    - Oumou's case was open for 10+ months because she required intensive support across multiple domains. Most child protection cases open and close within 3-6 months; trafficking survivors often need 12-18 months or longer. The case management infrastructure and funding must account for this reality. Premature case closure before sustainable recovery and protection is achieved re-exposes survivors to vulnerability.
