# CASE 007: Health Crisis - Diabetes Management in Migration

**Case Type:** Serious Illness & Chronic Condition Management  
**Child Profile:** Amir (age 8, Pakistani national)  
**Location:** Brussels, Belgium  
**Tuteur:** Sarah Dubois (Social Worker)  
**Duration:** 18 months  
**Status:** Ongoing with Stability

---

## NARRATIVE

Amir arrived at Brussels-Zaventem airport on a cold November morning, separated from his family. The 8-year-old had travelled from Lahore, Pakistan, with a distant aunt who abandoned him at the terminal, handing him to airport security with only a backpack and a handwritten note: "Please help him. He is diabetic and needs medicine."

The immediate crisis revealed layers of vulnerability. Amir was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes at age 6 in Pakistan, but his family could not afford consistent insulin access. The aunt, unauthorized to travel with him, hoped the Belgian child protection system would provide the medical care his family could not.

Sarah Dubois was assigned as Amir's tuteur following emergency placement in a pediatric foster home operated by Foyer Jeunesse. Her first week involved crisis stabilization: confirming Amir's medical history through Pakistani hospital records, establishing contact with endocrinologists, and navigating Belgium's healthcare system integration for undocumented minors.

**Medical Baseline**: Amir's blood glucose readings were dangerously erratic. His previous insulin regimen had been sporadic—three months of treatment, then two months without. He showed signs of early diabetic complications: slight nerve damage in his feet, elevated kidney markers, and chronic fatigue. He weighed only 18kg (well below normal for his age), suggesting malnutrition exacerbated the diabetes management challenge.

**Psychological Context**: Beyond the medical crisis, Amir was traumatized by abandonment and confused about his separation from his mother, brothers, and extended family. He spoke Urdu and some English; French was initially a barrier. He exhibited classic trauma responses: nightmares, refusal to eat certain foods (fearing they would "break his body"), and anxiety around medical procedures.

**System Navigation**: Sarah's first month required 47 separate interactions: hospital registrations, obtaining Amir's vaccination records from Lahore, coordinating with pediatric endocrinology, securing continuous glucose monitor (CGM) devices, setting up pharmacy accounts for insulin delivery, and enrolling him in school with medical accommodations.

By month three, Amir had stabilized medically. His HbA1c dropped from 9.8% to 7.2% with consistent insulin management, a CGM device, and nurse visits twice weekly at his foster home. Sarah also enrolled him in a children's diabetes support group at the Hôpital Universitaire Bruxelles (HUB), where he met Belgian children with similar conditions—reducing his isolation.

**Family Reunification Parallel**: By month six, Sarah initiated searches for Amir's biological family. Using Pakistani diaspora networks and the International Tracing Service, she located his mother, Fatima, in Lahore. Medical records confirmed Amir's family could not afford Type 1 diabetes care—insulin costs 50,000 PKR monthly (~€150), unaffordable for a single mother earning 10,000 PKR/month.

**Legal Complexity**: Belgium's laws allow asylum-based residency for children with serious medical conditions unavailable in their country of origin. Sarah worked with a Belgian immigration lawyer to establish that Amir's Type 1 diabetes qualified him for protective status, even without official asylum application from his family. This required medical evidence that Pakistani public healthcare (which covers insulin) had gaps in rural areas where his family lived.

**Emotional Reconciliation**: Months 8-12 involved video calls between Amir and his mother, facilitated by Sarah using WhatsApp translation tools. Amir's mother, Fatima, tearfully watched her son grow healthier in Belgium—something that would not have been possible in Pakistan. Sarah navigated the moral complexity: Amir had been abandoned, yet his abandonment may have saved his life.

**School & Social Integration**: Sarah enrolled Amir in a mainstream Belgian primary school (Ecole Belgique Primaire) with supports: a school nurse trained in diabetes management, dietary accommodations, and a classmate buddy system. By month 12, Amir had made friends, participated in school sports (soccer, with diabetes-safe hydration protocols), and began speaking French fluently.

**Crisis at Month 14**: Amir's aunt (the one who abandoned him) contacted authorities claiming to be his guardian and requesting custody and repatriation. Sarah's documentation of abandonment, Amir's medical needs, and his established stability in Belgium resulted in a family court decision granting Sarah expanded tuteur authority and denying the aunt's claim.

**Current Status (Month 18)**: Amir lives in a specialized foster home with nursing support. His medical condition is stable with HbA1c at 6.8%, he attends school, participates in diabetes camps, and has received a provisional residence permit valid until age 18. Sarah has initiated long-term medical planning, including potential pump therapy and structured family communication via social worker mediation.

## LEGAL FRAMEWORK APPLIED

1. **Belgium's Unaccompanied Minor Protection Law (2013)**
   - Automatic tuteur appointment upon arrival
   - Ensured Amir's right to medical care, education, housing
   - Sarah's legal authority to make medical decisions

2. **EU Directive 2005/71 (Healthcare Access for Irregular Migrants)**
   - Guaranteed emergency medical care regardless of documentation status
   - Enabled insulin access, endocrinology consultations, CGM devices
   - Overrode initial healthcare system barriers

3. **Belgium's Asylum Act (Medical Grounds for Subsidiary Protection)**
   - Established that serious medical condition unavailable in country of origin = grounds for protective status
   - Documented Pakistan's insulin access gaps in rural areas (Amir's origin district)
   - Created pathway for residence permit beyond age 18

4. **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**
   - Article 6: Right to life, survival, development
   - Article 9: Protection from separation from parents (unless necessary for child's best interest)
   - Supported both medical care and potential family reunification with safeguards

5. **Belgium's Family Code (Guardianship Provisions)**
   - Tuteur authority over medical decisions, education, property
   - Court override of aunt's custody claim based on abandonment evidence
   - Extended tuteur authority through adolescence

## LESSONS LEARNED

1. **Medical Complexity Requires Systems Literacy**
   - Chronic conditions like Type 1 diabetes demand coordination across multiple specialists. A tuteur must become fluent in healthcare system navigation, insurance regulations, and clinical terminology to advocate effectively. Sarah's breakthrough came when she obtained Amir's Pakistani medical records and could present complete clinical history to Belgian endocrinologists—this contextualized his needs and accelerated appropriate treatment.

2. **Trauma & Medical Care Are Inseparable**
   - Amir's abandonment trauma directly manifested in medical non-compliance patterns (refusing insulin out of control anxiety, hoarding food due to scarcity memories). Treating the illness without addressing the trauma was ineffective. Simultaneous psychological and medical intervention created exponential improvement.

3. **Family Reunification Requires Moral Nuance**
   - The family abandonment that traumatized Amir may have actually saved his life (he received medical care impossible in Pakistan). A tuteur's role is not to enforce simple "family reunification" but to navigate the messy reality: What is in the child's best interest? How do we honor family bonds while protecting health? Sarah facilitated contact without forcing repatriation, maintaining the child's agency.

4. **Legal Status Directly Enables Health Access**
   - Before asylum application, Amir's undocumented status created barriers: hospitals initially denied services; pharmacy coverage was limited. Once legal status clarified (medical necessity for protection), healthcare access became guaranteed, medications covered, specialist care available. Legal advocacy is healthcare advocacy for migrant children.

5. **Peer Connection Is Therapeutic**
   - Isolation deepened Amir's medical anxiety. Enrollment in diabetes support group (month 3) and school social integration (months 2-6) reduced psychological burden. For a child managing a chronic condition, peer validation ("other kids have this too") is often more powerful than adult reassurance.

6. **Language Access Determines Outcomes**
   - Amir's Urdu language preference initially created communication gaps with French-speaking psychologists, teachers, and healthcare providers. Investment in interpretation services and language tutoring (months 1-12) dramatically improved medical compliance, emotional expression, and school performance. Language is not a "nice-to-have"—it's foundational to child welfare.

7. **Foster Family Stability Matters as Much as Medical Care**
   - The initial general foster home provided adequate safety but lacked medical expertise. Transition to a specialized medical foster home (month 14) with nursing staff didn't change Amir's clinical management significantly, but it reduced emergency-response stress on his primary caregivers and created a more stable emotional environment. Stability enabled Amir to focus on growth rather than survival.

8. **Advocacy Against System Barriers Protects Children**
   - Multiple institutions initially tried to deny Amir care due to his undocumented status. Sarah's persistent advocacy—documenting medical necessity, engaging legal support, escalating through hospital ethics committees—overrode these barriers. A passive tuteur would have accepted "we can't serve undocumented children"; Sarah's activism secured that protection legally and practically.
