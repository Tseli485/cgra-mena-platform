# Guide to Collecting Real Cases: Ethics, Framework & Implementation

## Overview

This guide provides a comprehensive framework for ethically collecting, analyzing, and integrating real cases into the CGRA system. Real cases bring authentic context, cultural nuance, and practical wisdom that fictional cases cannot replicate. However, they require careful attention to consent, ethics, and security.

---

## Part 1: Consent & Ethics Framework

### 1.1 Legal & Regulatory Compliance

#### GDPR Requirements (EU & UK)
- **Lawful basis**: Obtain explicit consent or process under legitimate interest with clear privacy notice
- **Data Processing Agreement**: Required if working with third parties
- **Data Minimization**: Collect only essential case details
- **Subject Rights**: Participants can request access, correction, or deletion of their data
- **Data Retention**: Define retention periods (recommend 5-7 years for cases)
- **Breach Notification**: Must notify DPA and affected parties within 72 hours of breach

#### HIPAA Requirements (US Healthcare)
- **De-identification Standard**: Remove 18 HIPAA identifiers (name, address, dates, medical record numbers, etc.)
- **Expert Determination**: Alternative de-identification method requires statistical expert review
- **Business Associate Agreements**: Required for anyone handling PHI
- **Audit Trails**: Document all access to identified case data

#### Other Jurisdictions
- Check local data protection laws (LGPD in Brazil, PIPA in Canada, CCPA in California)
- Understand professional ethics codes (social work, counseling, psychology, medicine)
- Verify organizational IRB/ethics board requirements

### 1.2 Informed Consent Framework

#### Consent Document Elements
```
Essential components for consent form:

1. Clear Purpose Statement
   - "This case will be used to teach child safeguarding professionals"
   - Specific about intended audiences and contexts

2. Data Collection Scope
   - Exactly what information will be collected
   - How long it will be retained
   - Who will have access

3. Anonymization & Confidentiality
   - How the case will be anonymized
   - Residual re-identification risks
   - How data will be stored and secured

4. Right to Withdraw
   - Participants can withdraw consent anytime
   - Process for withdrawal and data deletion
   - Any limitations (e.g., already-published content)

5. Voluntary Participation
   - No pressure or coercion
   - Independent party obtains consent when possible
   - Clear statement: "Your care/services will continue regardless of consent"

6. Contact Information
   - Who to contact with questions
   - Data Protection Officer or equivalent
   - How to file complaints

7. Signature & Date
   - Digital or written signature
   - Participant retains copy
   - Second witness when appropriate
```

#### Special Considerations for Vulnerable Populations
- **Children & Youth**: Obtain dual consent (parent/guardian + minor when age-appropriate, typically 12+)
- **Incapacitated Individuals**: Use surrogate decision-maker or have ethics board waive consent
- **Minority Groups**: Ensure culturally appropriate language; consider community advisory boards
- **Imprisoned/Institutionalized**: Extra oversight; cannot condition services on participation
- **Economic Disadvantage**: Never offer financial incentives that constitute coercion

### 1.3 Professional Ethics Alignment

#### Duty of Confidentiality
- Maintain stricter confidentiality than legal requirements
- Document all access to identified case data
- Limit access to ethics-trained professionals
- Establish data governance committee

#### Cultural Sensitivity & Humility
- Partner with community representatives in case selection and presentation
- Review cultural assumptions embedded in analysis
- Ensure case framing doesn't reinforce stereotypes
- Acknowledge cultural variation in child rearing, family structure, help-seeking

#### Power Dynamics & Representation
- Avoid extractive research (taking from communities without benefit)
- Include voices of families and communities in case analysis
- Provide feedback/findings back to communities when appropriate
- Question who decides what counts as a "risk" or "success"

### 2.2 Form Guidance for Collectors

**Timeline Questions:**
- When did you become aware of this case? (marks start date)
- When was it active/being worked? (active period)
- When was it resolved, closed, or transferred? (marks end)
- How long ago? (helps determine freshness and recall reliability)

**Narrative Tips:**
- Write as if explaining to a peer who didn't know the case
- Include concrete details (behaviors, specific incidents, conversations) rather than only assessments
- Note who is talking about what (e.g., "Mother reported," "Youth stated," "School observed")
- Include what surprised you or what you learned

**Anonymization Confidence:**
- Could someone who knew the original family identify them? If yes, more anonymization needed
- Are combination of details (age + rare challenge + specific location) identifying? If yes, shift demographics
- Has identifying information (name, dates, school, case number) been removed? Must be yes

**Outcomes Clarity:**
- Not all cases have happy endings; document what actually happened
- If ongoing, explain what success would look like
- Follow-up data strengthens teaching value but isn't required

## Part 3: Analysis Framework

### 3.1 Applying the CGRA Structure to Real Cases

Real cases follow the same structure as fictional cases but with additional layers to honor complexity and authenticity.

#### Domain & Challenge Identification
```
For each case, identify:

Primary Domain: Which area does this case primarily address?
- Maltreatment, Neglect, Exploitation, Exposure, Mental Health, etc.
- Should match one of the 7 CGRA domains

Key Challenge: The core professional dilemma or question
- "How to assess risk when family culture differs from standards?"
- "How to engage youth who has experienced repeated betrayal?"
- "How to coordinate across systems when information isn't shared?"
- Should be specific, not generic ("child abuse" is too broad)

Intersecting Issues: What complexity makes this case real?
- Poverty, parental mental illness, discrimination, migration status
- These create the authentic texture that fictional cases can approximate
```

#### Lesson Extraction
```
Three levels of lesson depth (choose based on available detail):

1. Tactical Lesson (immediate application)
   "When a youth discloses abuse, validate before investigating"
   Action: specific technique or practice

2. Strategic Lesson (systemic understanding)
   "Sustainable change requires engaging families as partners, not clients"
   Action: approach shift or perspective change

3. Philosophical Lesson (deepens professional identity)
   "Child protection is an act of humility—acknowledging what we don't know"
   Action: internal posture or values alignment
```

#### Outcome Documentation
```
Record the actual outcome, not the ideal:

What Happened:
- Case closed → What conditions changed to allow closure?
- Case ongoing → What's the current status?
- Case escalated → What escalation and why?

Success Indicator:
- Not "child was safe" but "child attended school regularly, disclosed
  safety concerns, and family engaged with services"
- Measurable, observable
```

### 3.2 Handling Gaps & Limitations

Real cases often have incomplete data. Document honestly:

```
Data Quality Assessment:

Complete Information (80-100%):
→ Use for detailed scenario-based training

Partial Information (50-79%):
→ Use as illustrative case; acknowledge gaps in teaching
→ Example: "We don't know long-term outcomes, but here's what we learned"

Limited Information (<50%):
→ Use as brief case example or crisis snapshot
→ Focus on immediate decision-making moment
→ Example: "First contact decision-making under uncertainty"
```

### 3.3 Comparative Analysis Template

When adding real cases, use this to enhance teaching value:

```
How does this real case differ from the fictional version?

Fictional Version:
- Clearer causation (often simpler)
- Complete information (narrative closure)
- Typical presentation

Real Version:
- Ambiguous information (families unclear about history)
- Ongoing (no neat closure)
- Atypical elements (unusual combinations)

Teaching Implication:
- What does the real version teach that fiction didn't?
- Where was our assumption wrong?
- How should practice change based on real complexity?
```

## Part 4: Integration Workflow

### 4.1 Submission Process

```
Step 1: Case Collection & Anonymization (Collector)
├─ Complete Case Information Form
├─ Write detailed narrative
├─ Verify anonymization (no identifiers)
└─ Obtain all necessary consents

Step 2: Ethics Review (Ethics Committee or DPO)
├─ Review consent documentation
├─ Assess anonymization adequacy
├─ Identify re-identification risks
├─ Approve or request modifications
└─ Approve for system entry

Step 3: Case Analysis (Subject Matter Expert)
├─ Extract primary domain & challenge
├─ Identify key lesson
├─ Verify case quality (completeness, clarity)
├─ Map to existing fictional cases (if applicable)
└─ Note unique teaching value

Step 4: System Integration (Technical)
├─ Enter case into database
├─ Assign case ID (CGRA-REAL-XXX)
├─ Create case page (markdown)
├─ Link to related cases
└─ Generate metadata for search

Step 5: Training Review (Practitioners)
├─ Pilot case with training cohort
├─ Gather feedback on utility
├─ Identify presentation adjustments
└─ Document lessons learned

Step 6: Launch & Documentation
├─ Publish case with context notes
├─ Update master case table
├─ Add to navigation guide
└─ Archive consent & supporting docs separately
```

### 4.2 Quality Assurance Checklist

Before integrating a case into the system:

```
☐ Consent properly obtained and documented
☐ Anonymization verified (no identifiers remain)
☐ Narrative is clear and compelling
☐ Primary domain clearly identified
☐ Key challenge stated explicitly
☐ Key lesson extracted and verified
☐ Outcomes documented honestly
☐ Intersecting issues noted
☐ Cultural/contextual notes included
☐ Data completeness rated
☐ Staff confidence assessed
☐ No protected health information (PHI)
☐ Ethics review completed and approved
☐ Case tested in training context
☐ Ready for practitioner use
```

### 4.3 Version Control & Updates

Real cases may be updated over time as follow-up information becomes available:

```
Version Management:

Case v1.0 (Initial)
- Original submission
- Case snapshot at time of submission

Case v1.1 (Follow-up Update)
- 6-month or 1-year follow-up data
- Outcome updates
- Revised lesson based on time passage
- Note: retain v1.0 for teaching early decision-making

Case v2.0 (Significant New Information)
- Multi-year follow-up showing trajectory
- Complete outcome picture
- Potential lesson revision
- Can replace v1.0 or maintain as separate teaching tools
```

## Part 5: Validation Checklist

Use this comprehensive checklist to validate each real case before training use:

### 5.1 Ethical Validation

```
Consent & Legal
□ Informed consent obtained with documented agreement
□ Dual consent obtained where required (parent + child)
□ Consent form accessible to participant (copy provided)
□ Legal review completed (GDPR, HIPAA, local law)
□ Data processing agreement in place if needed
□ Ethics board approval obtained

Anonymization & Confidentiality
□ No real names or aliases used
□ No specific dates (use "6 months before case closure" format)
□ No specific locations beyond country/region
□ No organizational identifiers
□ Demographic details shifted if re-identification risk exists
□ Combination of identifiers cannot pinpoint individual
□ No sensitive health information that could identify
□ Digital and paper records stored separately
□ Access limited to authorized staff only

Participant Protection
□ No stigmatizing language ("troubled," "problem family")
□ Strengths and resilience highlighted alongside challenges
□ Family/youth voice represented, not just provider perspective
□ Cultural context respected and explained
□ Outcomes framed as action-dependent, not predetermined
□ No blame toward family or individual
□ Case respects dignity of all individuals
```

### 5.2 Content Validation

```
Case Quality
□ Narrative is compelling and specific
□ Timeline is clear (chronological or organized by decision point)
□ All key actors clearly identified by role (not name)
□ Motivations and perspectives included
□ Outcomes are realistic (not overly positive or negative)
□ Case is sufficiently detailed (not vague)
□ Case is sufficiently concise (not overwhelming)

Teaching Value
□ Primary domain clearly matches CGRA framework
□ Key challenge is genuinely ambiguous or difficult
□ Key lesson is non-obvious and valuable
□ Case teaches something new vs. existing cases
□ Intersecting issues add authentic complexity
□ Case is useful for training, not just interesting

Accuracy & Completeness
□ Case facts are accurate per available records
□ Gaps in information are acknowledged
□ Data quality rating is honest
□ Completeness is rated appropriately
□ Follow-up data is included if available
□ Staff confidence in account is realistic
```

### 5.3 Usability Validation

```
Training Readiness
□ Case has been piloted with a training cohort
□ Trainers found it effective for learning objectives
□ Practitioners found it realistic and relatable
□ Time required for case study is reasonable (15-45 min)
□ Reflection questions generate meaningful discussion
□ Case can be used at multiple experience levels
□ Case materials are accessible (clear language, short paragraphs)
□ Case connects to practical skills or knowledge

Documentation
□ Related cases are identified and linked
□ Metadata enables searching (keywords, domains, challenges)
□ Context notes clarify any unusual aspects
□ Trainer notes include discussion prompts
□ Participant guide includes reflection questions
□ Case references are complete and verified
□ Archives note where consent documents are stored
```

### 5.4 Security & Access Validation

```
Data Security
□ Identified case data stored in secure, encrypted system
□ Access logged and monitored
□ Access limited to ethics-trained professionals
□ De-identified case data accessible to trainers
□ Regular security audits performed
□ Breach protocol in place and tested
□ Data retention period clear and honored
□ Deletion procedures documented

Access Control
□ Clear distinction between identified and de-identified access
□ De-identified version used for training
□ Identified version accessed only for follow-up/ethics review
□ Dual password protection for identified records
□ Two-factor authentication for sensitive access
□ Access denied by default (whitelist approach)
□ Quarterly access reviews conducted
```

## Part 6: Common Ethical Challenges & Solutions

### Challenge 1: Family Doesn't Understand Purpose or May Be Harmed

**The Situation:**
Family agreed to participate but wasn't fully clear on "teaching practice to child protection professionals." Worry they may be harmed by seeing their case as a teaching example.

**Solutions:**
1. **Clarify Purpose in Consent:** Use specific language: "Other child protection workers will read your case to learn how to help families like yours better."
2. **Frame as Contribution:** "Your experience can help us train better professionals. The next family they work with may benefit."
3. **Emphasize Anonymization:** Explain specifically how identifying details will be removed (e.g., "Your name and location won't be used").
4. **Offer Review:** Provide family option to review anonymized case before it's used. Don't require it, but offer.
5. **Ongoing Communication:** Share back findings or lessons learned from the case analysis with family (if they want).

### Challenge 2: Staff Reluctant to Share Cases Due to Liability Concerns

**The Situation:**
Caseworker has great case but worries it might be used against them ("What if someone sues based on my decisions?")

**Solutions:**
1. **Legal Assurance:** Provide written statement from organizational counsel that case sharing is protected and won't be used as individual performance evidence.
2. **De-identification:** Make clear that identifying details will be removed, so case can't be traced to individual worker.
3. **Peer Review:** Involve case consultants or supervisors in case selection, positioning it as quality assurance activity.
4. **Training Purpose:** Frame as "we all learn from cases; sharing is professional development, not criticism."
5. **Confidentiality Clause:** Include in consent/submission that cases won't be used for disciplinary purposes.

### Challenge 3: Conflicting Perspectives (Family vs. Provider on "What Happened")

**The Situation:**
Case narrative from child welfare perspective shows parents as neglectful, but you learn family has perspective that context was poverty and cultural practices. Both perspectives are legitimate.

**Solutions:**
1. **Present Multiple Perspectives:** Include family perspective in case: "Parents explained that their practice of using home remedies was culturally normal in their country of origin."
2. **Contextualize Professional Assessment:** Help readers understand that professional concerns (e.g., delayed healthcare) are assessed against standards that may not account for cultural difference.
3. **Focus on Learning:** "What did we learn about asking questions before assuming neglect?" vs. "Were parents right or wrong?"
4. **Acknowledge Intersectionality:** Explain how poverty, discrimination, cultural difference, and professional standards all intersected in this case.
5. **Extract Humility Lesson:** "This case taught us to explore context before concluding harm was intentional."

### Challenge 4: Case Involves Very Serious Harm or Criminal Activity

**The Situation:**
Powerful teaching case but involves serious abuse, exploitation, or criminal prosecution. You worry about re-traumatization or victim safety.

**Solutions:**
1. **Assess Necessity:** Is this the best case to teach this lesson, or is there less harmful alternative?
2. **Get Victim/Survivor Input:** If appropriate and ethical, ask survivor for permission and input. They may have healing perspective.
3. **Trauma-Informed Presentation:** 
   - Warn trainers: "This case contains descriptions of serious harm. Recommend giving participants option to step out."
   - Provide grounding techniques before presentation
   - Focus on protective response rather than graphic details
4. **Anonymization is Essential:** Remove all identifying details. Consider composite cases if re-identification risk is high.
5. **Limit Details:** You can teach the lesson without graphic details. Example: Instead of describing abuse, say "The child experienced significant physical abuse. The question was whether..."
6. **Provide Resources:** Include mental health support information for trainers and participants affected by content.

### Challenge 5: Long-Term Outcome is Unknown or Uncertain

**The Situation:**
Case teaches a valuable lesson about early intervention, but you never followed up with the family. Teaching "what happened next" would be speculation.

**Solutions:**
1. **Honest About Limitation:** "This case was documented in 2022. We don't have current follow-up data."
2. **Document Your Last Known Information:** "As of case closure in 2022, the family was engaged with services and..."
3. **Reframe Teaching Value:** "What did we learn from this point of intervention?" rather than "was the outcome good?"
4. **Invite Speculation With Caution:** "What factors might influence how this case evolved?" encourages critical thinking without claiming certainty.
5. **Prioritize Cases With Follow-Up:** When possible, prioritize cases where you have longer-term information, but don't exclude cases without it.

### Challenge 6: Organizational Culture Doesn't Support Case Sharing

**The Situation:**
Organization has history of punitive use of cases ("we learned from your mistakes") or competitive dynamics ("don't share your cases, that makes us look bad").

**Solutions:**
1. **Leadership Buy-In:** Start with organizational leadership explaining why case sharing is institutional strength, not weakness.
2. **De-Link from Performance:** Establish explicit policy that cases are for learning, never for evaluation or discipline.
3. **Highlight Wins:** Celebrate cases of success or learning (staff solved a problem, family engaged, outcome improved).
4. **Make It Safe:** Invite cases anonymously if needed initially. Gradually build trust.
5. **Trainer Model:** Have respected trainers/leaders share their own cases first. Model vulnerability.
6. **Policy & Protection:** Document in writing that case sharing is protected and confidential.

### Challenge 7: Potential for Misuse (Training Context Changed)

**The Situation:**
Case was collected for "case conference training" but someone wants to use it in "investigations" or "hiring decisions" where context is different.

**Solutions:**
1. **Specify Approved Uses:** In case metadata, list approved uses: "Teaching ethical decision-making to caseworkers" vs. not approved for "performance evaluation."
2. **Access Controls:** Limit distribution to approved contexts. De-identified version for training; identified version only with ethics approval.
3. **Usage Agreement:** When cases are shared, require agreement that use will be limited to stated purpose.
4. **Ongoing Governance:** Have ethics committee review usage periodically. If misuse occurs, discontinue case sharing.
5. **Training for Trainers:** Educate all facilitators on ethical use: "This case should be used to build skill and empathy, not to criticize decisions."

## Part 7: Resource Protection

### 7.1 Data Security Framework

#### Storage & Encryption
```
Identified Case Data (with consent documents):
├─ Stored in encrypted database with dual password protection
├─ Access logs maintained (who, when, why)
├─ Access via VPN or secure connection only
├─ Regular security audits (quarterly minimum)
└─ Backup systems equally protected

De-Identified Case Data (for training):
├─ Stored in training management system (less restricted)
├─ Available to authorized trainers
├─ Still encrypted in transit
├─ Linked to identified version via encrypted key
└─ Audit logs track trainer access

Consent Documents:
├─ Stored separately from case data (different systems)
├─ Paper copies in locked cabinet
├─ Digital copies in separate encrypted system
├─ Access extremely limited (ethics staff only)
└─ Retention per legal/organizational requirements
```

#### Access Control Model
```
Tier 1 - De-Identified Case (Training)
├─ Access: All trainers and authorized training staff
├─ Authentication: Single password or organizational login
├─ Logging: Basic access logging
└─ Purpose: Teaching case scenarios

Tier 2 - Identified Case (Quality Assurance)
├─ Access: Ethics committee, DPO, case auditors
├─ Authentication: Dual password + second factor
├─ Logging: Detailed access logging with reasons
└─ Purpose: Ethics review, re-identification risk assessment

Tier 3 - Consent & Sensitive Documents
├─ Access: DPO, ethics committee chair, organizational counsel
├─ Authentication: Dual factor + physical access control
├─ Logging: Every access logged and reviewed
└─ Purpose: Legal compliance, ethics review only
```

### 7.2 Confidentiality Protocols

#### Information Handling
```
Rule 1: Never mix identified and de-identified data
├─ Case narrative stored de-identified
├─ Identifying details stored in separate encryption key
├─ Cannot reconstitute full identified case without both
└─ Database enforces separation

Rule 2: Minimize duration of identification
├─ Case identified only during ethics review
├─ Converted to de-identified for storage
├─ Re-identified only if needed for follow-up
└─ Time-limited access for any identification

Rule 3: Minimize recipients of identifying information
├─ Original consent holder (family)
├─ Collector/submitter (for accountability)
├─ Ethics reviewer (for adequacy assessment)
├─ DPO (for legal compliance)
└─ No other staff need access to identified data

Rule 4: Document all disclosure
├─ Every time identified data is accessed, log it
├─ Include: who, when, why, duration
├─ Review logs monthly for unusual patterns
└─ Flag any access that exceeds stated purpose
```

#### Training on Confidentiality
```
Required for all staff with access to cases:
├─ Data protection fundamentals (GDPR, HIPAA, local law)
├─ What constitutes a breach and how to report it
├─ Secure communication (no unencrypted email)
├─ Clean desk policy (no printed cases left visible)
├─ Password management and security
├─ Consequences of breach (legal, organizational, disciplinary)
└─ Annual recertification required
```

### 7.3 Breach Response Protocol

#### If Breach is Suspected
```
Immediate (within 24 hours):
├─ Notify data protection officer immediately
├─ Contain the breach (shut down access if needed)
├─ Document exactly what information may have been exposed
├─ Preserve all evidence (logs, emails, etc.)
└─ Initiate investigation

Assessment (within 48-72 hours):
├─ Determine scope (how many individuals affected?)
├─ Assess risk level (high, moderate, low likelihood of harm)
├─ Identify all affected individuals
├─ Determine whether notification is required by law
└─ Identify root cause

Notification (per legal timeline, usually 72 hours for GDPR):
├─ Notify Data Protection Authority
├─ Notify affected individuals unless low risk
├─ Provide information about breach and response
├─ Offer remediation (credit monitoring, etc., if applicable)
└─ Maintain documentation for regulatory review

Remediation & Prevention:
├─ Fix the vulnerability that caused the breach
├─ Implement additional security measures
├─ Provide staff training on what went wrong
├─ Review and strengthen protocols
└─ Conduct follow-up audit
```

### 7.4 Retention & Deletion Policies

#### Case Retention Schedule
```
Consent Documents:
├─ Retain for legal minimum (usually 3-5 years)
├─ Retain 1 year after case is no longer in use
├─ Destroy after retention period per secure deletion protocol
└─ Consider longer retention for high-impact cases

Identified Case Data:
├─ Retain while case is actively used for training
├─ Retain 1 year after case is withdrawn from use
├─ Destroy per secure deletion protocol
└─ Exception: may retain longer if participant requests follow-up

De-Identified Case Data:
├─ Retain indefinitely (de-identification is permanent)
├─ No requirement to delete once de-identified
├─ Consider long-term value for future training
└─ Review periodically for ongoing appropriateness

Access Logs:
├─ Retain for 3 years (compliance requirement)
├─ Analyze for patterns or unusual access
├─ Delete after retention period
└─ Never delete during pending investigation
```

#### Secure Deletion Process
```
For Paper Records:
├─ Shred (cross-cut shredder, not straight cut)
├─ Or burn (if permitted and secure)
├─ Obtain certificate of destruction
└─ Document deletion in records

For Digital Records:
├─ Use secure deletion software (not just delete)
├─ Overwrite data multiple times to prevent recovery
├─ Verify deletion with forensic tools
├─ Document deletion with timestamp and verification
└─ Maintain audit trail showing deletion occurred
```
