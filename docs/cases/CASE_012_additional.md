# CASE 012: Additional Complex Case - Legal, Employment & Family Intersections

**Child Profile:**
- Name: Bashir M.
- Nationality: Sudan
- Age: 15 years old
- Gender: Male
- Location: Khartoum, Sudan
- Case Status: Active intervention (12 months)
- Tuteur: Mme. Farah Hassan (senior child protection officer)
- Date Case Opened: January 10, 2026

---

## Story and Narrative

Bashir is 15 years old and already lives at the intersection of multiple legal, economic, and family crises that illustrate how systemic vulnerabilities compound during adolescence. He was identified as requiring child protection intervention in January 2026 after his school reported suspected child labor and a concerning family situation.

Bashir was born to Amal, a divorced woman with no stable income. His father, Ibrahim, remarried years ago and had minimal contact with Bashir. His mother's second husband, Karim, is an intermittently employed mechanic who has had a volatile and sometimes abusive relationship with Amal. Bashir has two younger half-siblings from Karim (ages 6 and 4).

For much of his childhood, Bashir attended school and lived in relative stability with his mother, father, and extended family. Around age 12, the family situation deteriorated. Amal lost her job as a cleaner; Karim's work became increasingly irregular. The family fell into severe poverty. At age 13, Bashir stopped attending school regularly. By 14, he had withdrawn from school entirely.

Instead of school, Bashir began working in an informal auto repair shop owned by a distant relative, Mustafa. The work paid small irregular sums (roughly $3-5 per day) and required 10-12 hour days. The work involved heavy machinery, toxic chemicals, and no safety equipment. At age 14, Bashir sustained a serious hand injury (laceration requiring stitches) working in unsafe conditions. The injury was never properly treated and became infected.

Around the same time, tensions in Bashir's household escalated. Karim, frustrated with poverty and joblessness, became verbally abusive toward Amal and physically abusive toward the children, including Bashir. When Bashir returned from work injured and in pain, Karim blamed him for being careless. An argument escalated, and Karim hit Bashir with a belt, worsening the injury. Amal, unable to afford medical care, initially tried home treatment. The infection worsened; Bashir developed fever and signs of systemic illness.

A neighbor, concerned about Bashir's visible injuries and illness, contacted local authorities. The police investigation revealed multiple issues: child labor in hazardous conditions, physical abuse by stepfather, medical neglect, and family violence. Bashir was referred to child protection services. Mme. Farah Hassan was assigned as his tuteur.

The case is extraordinarily complex. Legally, it involves child labor, abuse, and potential prosecution of the stepfather. Medically, it requires treating an infected hand injury that developed during unsafe labor. Educationally, Bashir had lost two years of schooling and is far behind his age cohort; he cannot return to standard Grade 10. Economically, the family's survival depends on Bashir's and his mother's income, yet his work violates child labor laws. Socially, Bashir is isolated from peers and school relationships. Legally, there are questions about prosecution of the employer (the relative), the stepfather, the mother's accountability for allowing the situation.

Over 12 months, intervention has required: medical treatment of the injury and infection, psychological support for trauma and loss of childhood to labor, family assessment and safety planning, educational catch-up, legal navigation of child labor and abuse cases, economic restructuring to reduce child labor dependence, and coordination across multiple sectors. The case reveals how adolescent vulnerability differs from younger children's—Bashir has agency and opinions about his situation, he is engaged in economically productive work (however exploitative), his body is developing and injuries may have permanent consequences, and his educational delay is now very difficult to reverse.

## Legal Framework Applied

1. **Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**
   - Article 6: Right to life, survival, and development
   - Article 19: Protection from violence, abuse, and neglect
   - Article 28: Right to education
   - Article 29: Education directed toward development of child's potential
   - Article 32: Protection from economic exploitation and harmful work
   - Article 33: Protection from drug abuse and trafficking
   - Article 34: Protection from sexual abuse
   - Article 36: Protection from other forms of exploitation
   - Article 37: Protection from torture and cruel, inhuman treatment

2. **Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW)**
   - Article 5: Elimination of gender stereotypes affecting women and girls
   - Article 11: Right to work, equal pay, and protection from discrimination
   - Article 12: Access to health care and medical services
   - Article 16: Equality in family law; protection from domestic violence

3. **ILO Convention 182: Worst Forms of Child Labor**
   - Hazardous work is a worst form of child labor
   - Machinery operation without safety is specifically prohibited
   - Exposure to toxic chemicals is prohibited
   - Requirements for immediate action, withdrawal from work, and remedial services
   - Education and rehabilitation services required

4. **ILO Convention 138: Minimum Age for Work**
   - Minimum age for work is 15 (or 14 in developing countries)
   - Bashir's age 13-14 labor violates even developing country exceptions
   - Requirements for regulation and monitoring of child work

5. **African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child (ACRWC)**
   - Article 1: Child defined as anyone under 18
   - Article 5: Right to dignity, respect, and survival
   - Article 11: Education as right; prohibition of harmful practices
   - Article 15: Protection from economic exploitation
   - Article 16: Protection from all forms of violence, abuse, and neglect
   - Article 20: Parental responsibilities and state support for families
   - Article 21: Protection and assistance for children separated from families

6. **Sudan National Legislation**
   - Sudan Child Act (2010): Rights and protection of children; definition of child as under 18
   - Labor laws: Prohibition of child labor below age 15 in most sectors
   - Hazardous work prohibited for anyone under 18
   - Criminal Code: Provisions against assault, abuse, and violence
   - Domestic violence legislation (evolving legal framework)
   - Education law: Compulsory primary education (ages 6-13)

7. **Occupational Safety and Health Standards**
   - Sudanese labor law: requirements for workplace safety
   - International Labor Organization standards for hazardous work prevention
   - Requirements for protective equipment, training, and safe conditions
   - Employer liability for occupational injuries

## Lessons Learned

1. **Adolescent Vulnerability is Qualitatively Different from Younger Children**
   - Bashir left school and entered labor at age 13—at a point when he had some understanding of his situation but limited ability to change it. Adolescents are not small adults; they are not yet financially independent, legally autonomous, or fully developmentally mature. But they also have agency, voice, and opinions that must be respected. The tuteur must balance protection (which younger children may accept passively) with respect for Bashir's developing autonomy. Listening to his views on education, work, family, and future is essential—not just making decisions for him.

2. **Poverty-Driven Child Labor is Systematic, Not Just Individual**
   - Bashir didn't choose labor; his family chose it because they would starve without his income. The employer (Mustafa) knowingly exploited a child because it was cheaper and easier than hiring an adult. Neither Bashir nor his family nor even his mother fully "chose" this situation—it was the consequence of economic systems that leave families with no alternatives. Intervention that removes child labor without addressing family poverty simply replicates it. The case required simultaneous action on: child's protection (removal from labor), family's economic survival (government assistance), and mother's employment (job support).

3. **Occupational Injuries in Child Labor Have Permanent Consequences**
   - Bashir's hand injury happened because of unsafe conditions; the infection developed because of poverty preventing medical care. Now at age 15, he has permanent residual scarring and slight loss of fine motor control. At adolescence, when life trajectory is being determined, such injuries can redirect educational and occupational possibilities. Prevention of occupational injuries in children is not just about ethics—it's about preventing permanent disability that compromises futures.

4. **Domestic Violence Against Mothers Affects Child Protection**
   - Amal wanted to protect Bashir but was herself trapped by intimate partner violence and economic dependence. Her inability to protect was not primarily a failure of parenting—it was a consequence of her own victimization and powerlessness. Protecting Bashir required simultaneously protecting Amal: ensuring her economic independence, supporting her safety, and removing the abuser. Child protection that ignores mothers' own abuse is incomplete and fails the whole family.

5. **Educational Delay is Cumulative and Difficult to Reverse**
   - Bashir left school at age 13, having completed Grade 6 or 7. At age 15, even after intensive catch-up education, he cannot return to Grade 10 (his age-appropriate grade). The gap is permanent. Even if Bashir succeeds in vocational education and gains employment, he will never have the education of his age cohort. Lost schooling during early adolescence has lifelong consequences. Early identification and intervention—before the two-year gap occurs—is essential. Prevention is far easier than remediation.

6. **Legal Processes Are Slow and Can Re-Traumatize Victims**
   - Bashir's case involves potential prosecution of two parties (employer Mustafa and stepfather Karim). A year into the case, prosecutions are still ongoing. Bashir's participation (or potential participation) in legal proceedings risks re-traumatization. The promise of "justice" (prosecution, consequences) is important but uncertain and slow. The tuteur must manage expectations: prosecution may never happen, sentences may be light, and the process itself can be difficult. Victim's recovery must not be entirely dependent on legal outcomes.

7. **Adolescent Identity Formation is Disrupted by Trauma and Labor**
   - A normal 13-15 year old is developing identity: exploring peers, education, interests, future possibilities. Bashir's adolescence was spent in labor and fear. Even after intervention, he must re-engage with adolescent development tasks at a later point. The counseling and educational support must explicitly address identity reconstruction: helping Bashir understand who he is beyond "laborer" or "abused child," and helping him imagine future possibilities.

8. **Economic Support Must Be Coupled with Employment Support for Caregivers**
   - Emergency cash assistance was necessary to stop the family's immediate economic crisis. But if it ended there, Bashir's family would face poverty again. The assistance was sustainable only because it was paired with mother Amal's job search support and successful (partial) employment. Economic sustainability requires building caregiver's employment capacity, not just providing temporary transfers.

9. **Prosecutorial Response to Child Labor Requires Multi-Sectoral Coordination**
   - Investigating and prosecuting child labor involves police, labor authorities, prosecutors, and child protection services. If these systems don't coordinate, the investigation may be weak, evidence lost, or perpetrators not held accountable. Bashir's tuteur had to actively coordinate with police, ensure evidence preservation, and follow up on prosecution progress. Passive assumption that authorities will handle it often fails; active case management coordination is essential.

10. **Recovery from Simultaneous Traumas Takes Time and Patience**
    - Bashir experienced abuse trauma, medical trauma, economic trauma, and identity disruption. Healing across all these domains doesn't happen quickly. At 12 months, Bashir is on a positive trajectory: medically stable, psychologically improving, educationally engaged, family safe. But full recovery may take years. The tuteur's role includes maintaining hope, celebrating incremental progress, and continuing support even when crisis phase has passed. Case closure should not happen prematurely, and continued monitoring is essential.

## Checklist for Complex Adolescent Cases (Labor + Abuse + Family + Education + Legal)

### Immediate Assessment and Safety Planning (Week 1-2)
- [ ] Comprehensive safety assessment; immediate risks identified
- [ ] Medical assessment; any injuries documented and treated
- [ ] Psychological assessment; trauma and mental health baseline
- [ ] Educational assessment; academic level and gaps identified
- [ ] Family assessment; relationships, violence, economic situation
- [ ] Adolescent's understanding and preferences documented
- [ ] Legal issues identified; reporting to authorities initiated
- [ ] Sheltering decision made if needed; safety plan developed
- [ ] All findings documented comprehensively; integrated case plan begun

### Medical Care and Occupational Injury Treatment (Ongoing)
- [ ] Emergency medical care provided; injury assessment documented
- [ ] Infection assessment and treatment initiated; antibiotics if needed
- [ ] Pain management; comfort measures in place
- [ ] Nutritional support for healing
- [ ] Occupational/physical therapy referral made
- [ ] Regular monitoring of healing; weekly assessments initially
- [ ] Wound documentation for legal proceedings
- [ ] Long-term prognosis assessment; permanent damage evaluation
- [ ] Rehabilitation plan for functional recovery
- [ ] Connection to ongoing health care; follow-up ensured

### Psychological Support and Trauma Processing (Ongoing, Intensive)
- [ ] Referral to trauma-informed child/adolescent psychologist
- [ ] Initial psychosocial first aid; safety and trust building
- [ ] Trauma assessment; PTSD and complex trauma evaluated
- [ ] Therapy modality selected: TF-CBT, play therapy, other
- [ ] Regular sessions scheduled; 2x weekly initially
- [ ] Processing of abuse trauma, injury trauma, identity disruption
- [ ] Depression screening and intervention if needed
- [ ] Coping skills development; stress management
- [ ] Suicide/self-harm risk assessment; safety planning if needed
- [ ] Coordination with school and family regarding trauma needs

### Safety and Sheltering (Immediate to 1-3 months)
- [ ] Home environment safety assessment; risks documented
- [ ] Decision on sheltering; protective placement if needed
- [ ] Safe facility identified; trauma-informed care from first contact
- [ ] Ongoing safety monitoring; regular reassessment
- [ ] Abuser identification and removal/arrest coordination
- [ ] Transition planning; gradual return to family as safety improves
- [ ] Safety planning for return home; protocols for protection
- [ ] Contact maintenance with family during sheltering
- [ ] Other children in household assessed for safety

### Abuse Investigation and Prosecution Support (Ongoing)
- [ ] Police report filed for assault/abuse
- [ ] Investigation coordination; evidence gathering
- [ ] Medical documentation provided to investigators
- [ ] Witness statements collected
- [ ] Criminal charges filed against abuser
- [ ] Victim support services engaged; Bashir informed of process
- [ ] Preparation for potential testimony (trauma-informed)
- [ ] Legal representation or advocacy support accessed
- [ ] Monitoring of case progress; follow-up if stalled
- [ ] Victim protection measures if perpetrator threatening

### Child Labor Investigation and Prosecution (Ongoing)
- [ ] Labor authority notification; child labor investigation initiated
- [ ] Workplace assessment; hazardous conditions documented
- [ ] Employer identification and investigation
- [ ] Evidence of child labor gathered; documentation secured
- [ ] Criminal or administrative charges filed against employer
- [ ] Inspection of workplace; safety violations documented
- [ ] Other child laborers identified; referral for protection
- [ ] Monitoring for employer retaliation or threats
- [ ] Employer accountability pursued through legal channels

### Educational Assessment and Re-engagement (Week 2-4)
- [ ] Educational assessment; current level determined
- [ ] Grade-level comparison; identification of gaps
- [ ] Learning style assessment; cognitive functioning evaluated
- [ ] Non-standard education pathway identified (vocational, catch-up)
- [ ] Non-formal education program enrollment; basic skills focus
- [ ] Educational plan developed; realistic timeline
- [ ] School identified for formal enrollment; coordination regarding needs
- [ ] Peer reintegration support; social preparation
- [ ] Regular monitoring of attendance and engagement
- [ ] Additional support arranged: tutoring, literacy specialists

### Family Assessment and Strengthening (Week 2-6)
- [ ] Comprehensive family assessment; composition and relationships
- [ ] Mother's mental health and wellbeing assessed
- [ ] Mother's capacity for child protection; strengths and challenges
- [ ] Family members' roles in support; extended family engagement
- [ ] Father's (Ibrahim) capacity and willingness assessed
- [ ] Family's understanding of labor exploitation and abuse
- [ ] Psychoeducation provided; expectations for recovery set
- [ ] Individual counseling for mother; addressing her own trauma
- [ ] Parenting support program; trauma-informed approaches
- [ ] Family meetings facilitated; healthy communication supported

### Domestic Violence Response (If applicable)
- [ ] Domestic violence screening; mother's situation assessed
- [ ] Safety assessment for mother; risk level documented
- [ ] Safety planning if mother remains in abusive relationship
- [ ] Domestic violence resources provided; counseling referral
- [ ] Shelter options discussed if mother's safety threatened
- [ ] Coordinated community response if available
- [ ] Child protection integrated with domestic violence response
- [ ] Monitoring of mother's safety; escalation if danger increases
- [ ] Economic support to increase mother's independence

### Economic Assessment and Support (Week 3-6)
- [ ] Household economic situation comprehensively assessed
- [ ] Income sources and stability documented
- [ ] Expenses and priorities identified
- [ ] Poverty level determination; assistance eligibility
- [ ] Government emergency assistance identified; applications completed
- [ ] Cash transfer or assistance approved and monitored
- [ ] Mother's employment barriers identified; job search support
- [ ] Job training or placement services accessed
- [ ] Microfinance or self-employment assessment if appropriate
- [ ] Budgeting and financial literacy support
- [ ] Monitoring for return to child labor due to poverty

### Multi-Sectoral Coordination and Case Management (Ongoing)
- [ ] Case team established; roles and responsibilities defined
- [ ] Contacts with all service providers: medical, school, legal, etc.
- [ ] Multi-disciplinary case conferences scheduled monthly
- [ ] Information-sharing agreements; confidentiality protocols
- [ ] Integrated case plan with shared goals
- [ ] Regular communication from all providers
- [ ] Service gaps identified and addressed
- [ ] Conflicts between providers resolved
- [ ] Comprehensive case documentation maintained
- [ ] Adolescent and family's participation in planning

### Adolescent Engagement and Agency (Ongoing)
- [ ] Adolescent's voice and preferences centered in decisions
- [ ] Understanding of situation and future possibilities discussed
- [ ] Agency and capability recognized; involvement in planning
- [ ] Peer support and mentoring relationships developed
- [ ] Future planning discussions; education and employment exploration
- [ ] Identity reconstruction supported; moving beyond "laborer" identity
- [ ] Self-advocacy skills developed; empowerment prioritized
- [ ] Peer relationships reestablished; social engagement
- [ ] Resilience building; strengths and capabilities emphasized

### Monitoring and Outcomes (Regular, Ongoing)
- [ ] Health outcomes tracked: injury healing, functional recovery
- [ ] Psychological outcomes monitored: trauma symptoms, depression
- [ ] Educational outcomes assessed: attendance, grades, engagement
- [ ] Family safety and relationship quality monitored
- [ ] Economic stability tracked
- [ ] Legal case progress followed
- [ ] Child and family's wellbeing prioritized in monitoring
- [ ] Progress celebrated; challenges addressed promptly
- [ ] Protective factors strengthened; vulnerabilities reduced
- [ ] Regular case review and plan adjustment

### Case Transition and Closure Planning (Month 9-12)
- [ ] Medical treatment complete; ongoing care plan established
- [ ] Psychological recovery adequate; ongoing therapy plan if needed
- [ ] Educational program sustainable; school enrollment stable
- [ ] Family safety stable; protective relationships secure
- [ ] Economic situation stabilized; sustainable income identified
- [ ] Abuser removed or separated; ongoing threat assessment
- [ ] Legal cases resolved or on clear trajectory
- [ ] Family's independent functioning capacity assessed
- [ ] Monitoring plan developed for post-closure period
- [ ] Clear re-opening criteria defined if situation deteriorates
- [ ] Formal closure meeting; all parties informed and agreement obtained

### Post-Closure Monitoring (6-12 months)
- [ ] Regular check-ins with family; frequency based on risk
- [ ] Educational engagement and progress continued
- [ ] Family stability and safety maintained
- [ ] Economic situation sustained
- [ ] Psychological wellbeing maintained; access to support if crisis
- [ ] Medical follow-up for injury completed
- [ ] Monitoring for recurrence of child labor or abuse
- [ ] Rapid response if risks re-emerge; case reopened if needed
- [ ] Celebration of family's progress and resilience
