# CASE 003: Severe Trauma and PTSD - Congolese Minor

## Story/Narrative

Karim was nine years old when he was rescued from an armed group in the Democratic Republic of Congo. He had been with them for six months—six months that he could barely articulate, even months later to the therapists who would try to help him.

He came from Bukavu, a city on Lake Kivu where his family lived in relative security compared to other parts of DRC. His father was a teacher, his mother worked in a clinic. Karim had a sister, Amelia, who was seven. He was a smart, curious boy who liked mathematics and drawing. He collected beautiful stones from the lakeshore and had a small notebook where he drew pictures of birds.

In March 2024, during a period of escalating conflict, armed men came to his neighborhood. He doesn't remember exactly what happened, only pieces: the sound of gunfire, his mother pushing him behind a door, running. He was separated from his family in the chaos. He ran into a crowd, then through streets he didn't know, and eventually into a building with other children. He was confused, terrified, and nobody seemed to know what to do with him.

Over the next hours, he learned he had been taken by a militia group. He learned to be invisible, to make no noise, to obey without question. The group kept him and fifteen other children in a compound. Some of the older boys were given weapons and training. Karim was too young for that, but he was made to help with tasks—carrying supplies, preparing food, doing whatever he was told. He was hit several times when he moved too slowly or asked questions. He saw violence against other children.

He doesn't talk much about the sexual abuse. Even now, eight months after rescue, he can barely acknowledge it happened. He shuts down if anyone mentions his body or touches him unexpectedly. He has nightmares where he wakes screaming. Sometimes during the day, a sound or a smell triggers a flashback, and he becomes rigid, his eyes unfocused, unreachable.

He was rescued in September 2024 when a humanitarian organization working with UNICEF negotiated with the militia. He was taken to a medical facility where he was found to be malnourished, covered in scars and bruises, and showing severe signs of psychological trauma. His name was recorded as "Karim," but they weren't certain that was actually his name—he had been too frightened to speak clearly at first. They had no documentation of his identity, no information about his family.

For weeks after rescue, Karim barely ate or spoke. He would hide under blankets. He would rock back and forth. The medical staff struggled to help him. He needed physical healing—treatment for infections, nutritional rehabilitation, medical care for various injuries. But what he needed most seemed unreachable: safety, security, and recovery from trauma that had rewired his developing brain.

A trauma specialist named Dr. James was brought in through the International Rescue Committee. Dr. James had worked with refugee children from many countries and understood that healing trauma, especially childhood trauma, requires patience, presence, and a completely different approach than standard counseling. He began with very simple things: spending time in the same room with Karim without making demands. He would sit nearby while Karim drew. He would ask permission before touching anything. He modeled safety through consistency and respect of Karim's boundaries.

Over weeks, Karim began to trust Dr. James enough to communicate through drawings. He couldn't say what had happened in words, but his pictures told stories—stick figures with big red mouths screaming, dark clouds, weapons. Dr. James never pushed for information. He validated every drawing and every emotion. Slowly, Karim began to use words again. His vocabulary was limited by trauma—he often couldn't remember words he had known before, or he would use words incorrectly. But he spoke.

The question of Karim's family was complicated. The organization tried to locate them through ICRC (International Committee of Red Cross) and local contacts in Bukavu, but communications in the region were difficult. There was no certainty they were alive, no way to know if they would come looking for him. The assumption was that Karim would need to be placed in a transitional care facility, then potentially resettled.

But then, in November 2024, a breakthrough: a social worker in Bukavu identified a woman named Marie who might be Karim's mother's sister. The woman confirmed she knew a family matching Karim's family's description, but she had no recent contact. Through Red Cross tracing, they eventually established that Karim's mother was alive and had been searching for him. His father had been killed during the initial violence. His sister, Amelia, was with his mother.

Reconnection, however, was not simple. Karim's mother, Amara, had undergone her own trauma—loss of her husband, months of searching for her son, fear he was dead. When she saw him on a video call arranged through ICRC, she wept so hard she couldn't speak. Karim's reaction was more muted. He recognized her face but seemed confused, like she was from another life. He was afraid.

A plan was made for repatriation to DRC, but this required careful management. Karim was not psychologically ready for the journey. He was still deeply traumatized, vulnerable, and his nervous system was only beginning to regulate. A psychologist specialized in trauma counseled that moving him too quickly could be re-traumatizing. His mother was told this would take time.

Dr. James arranged for intensive trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT), adapted for children. The therapy was gradual and paced, allowing Karim to process small pieces of trauma while remaining in a safe environment. He was also prescribed medication for his nightmares and anxiety, prescribed by a psychiatrist who specialized in pediatric trauma. His physical health—nutrition, growth, medical follow-up—was carefully monitored.

After three months of intensive therapy and preparation, Karim was ready for a video call reunion with his mother and sister. Watching him see his mother's face and say "Mama" was transformative for everyone present. Over the next two months, visits increased—more calls, messages relayed through the organization, his mother's voice on recording saying she loved him and was waiting for him.

In February 2025, Karim made the journey home. It was done slowly, with support. Dr. James arranged for a social worker from ICRC to accompany him. His mother had been trained in trauma-informed parenting through an organization working in DRC. The reunification was not a fairy tale ending—Karim still had nightmares, still struggled with trust and physical touch. But he was home, with his mother and sister. And he was healing.

## Practitioner Responsibilities (Dr. James and Team)

1. **Trauma Assessment and Documentation** - Conducted comprehensive trauma history (non-invasive); documented symptoms; created baseline for progress tracking
2. **Presence and Safety Creation** - Modeled safety through consistency, respect, and patience; spent hundreds of hours simply being present without demands
3. **Therapeutic Relationship Building** - Established the crucial relationship that would allow healing; earned trust through actions not words
4. **Art and Non-Verbal Therapy Facilitation** - Provided art materials and space for expression; interpreted drawings respectfully; validated emotion
5. **Trauma-Focused CBT Implementation** - Trained in specialized protocol for children; carefully paced narrative exposure; managed emotional activation
6. **Psychiatric Collaboration** - Coordinated with psychiatrist for medication management; monitored side effects; adjusted treatment as needed
7. **Family Tracing and Contact** - Worked with Red Cross to locate family; facilitated initial communications; managed reunion process
8. **Medical Coordination** - Liaised with medical team; ensured psychological needs integrated with medical care; holistic approach
9. **Repatriation Preparation** - Prepared Karim emotionally and practically for journey home; arranged support for travel; planned post-return services
10. **Mother Education and Support** - Taught Amara about trauma; explained Karim's needs; provided ongoing consultation; supported her own healing

## Lessons Learned

1. **Relationship is the Primary Intervention** - Healing from trauma first requires safety in relationship; therapeutic techniques are secondary to being present with the child
2. **Non-Verbal Expression is Essential** - Children traumatized into silence need art, play, and movement; words come later, after safety is established
3. **Pacing is Critical** - Pushing trauma disclosure too quickly causes re-traumatization; slow, steady, child-led pace allows integration and healing
4. **Bodily Safety Comes First** - Physical health (nutrition, sleep, medicine) must be addressed before or alongside psychological work
5. **Patience Rewires Trauma** - Consistent, patient presence over time literally changes brain physiology in traumatized children
6. **Family Reunion is Complex** - Both child and family members are traumatized; reunion must be carefully managed to avoid secondary trauma
7. **Local Context Matters** - Healing must include cultural, community, and family dimensions; external therapy alone is insufficient
8. **Hope Must Be Maintained** - Karim's connection to his mother—even through brief messages—sustained him through darkest periods of therapy

## Checklist for Severe Trauma in Children

- [ ] Immediate medical assessment; treat life-threatening conditions first
- [ ] Secure, safe environment away from threatening individuals/situations
- [ ] Basic physiological needs met (food, water, hygiene, sleep)
- [ ] Assign trauma-specialized mental health provider (not just any counselor)
- [ ] Establish relationship through presence, not interrogation or demands
- [ ] Conduct trauma screening; document baseline symptoms and responses
- [ ] Identify whether child can be non-verbal or needs alternative expression (art, play, movement)
- [ ] Do not force narrative disclosure; allow expression at child's pace
- [ ] Coordinate medical and psychological care; ensure integration
- [ ] Screen for dissociation, self-harm, suicidal ideation; monitor closely
- [ ] Teach grounding techniques; help child return to present moment
- [ ] If medication indicated, start low-dose, monitor carefully, use as tool not replacement
- [ ] Locate family; assess safety of reunification; proceed cautiously
- [ ] Train family members in trauma-informed responses before reunion
- [ ] Plan phased return to family/home if appropriate; don't rush
- [ ] Establish long-term follow-up care; healing takes years, not months
- [ ] Address educational needs; learning capacity affected by trauma
- [ ] Celebrate small wins; acknowledge the courage it takes to heal
- [ ] Understand re-traumatization risks; adjust environment/approach accordingly
- [ ] Document everything; detailed records inform ongoing care and legal protection
