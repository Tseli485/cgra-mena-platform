# How to Use CGRA Cases: Navigation & Application Guide

A practical guide for finding, understanding, and learning from child safeguarding cases. Whether you're new to the field or an experienced practitioner, this guide shows you how to get the most from the case collection.

---

## Quick Start (5 minutes)

### I need a case for a specific situation. What do I do?

1. **Identify your challenge**: What are you trying to learn about?
   - Assessment (deciding if something is abuse)
   - Engagement (building trust with families)
   - Decision-making (placement, service intensity)
   - Trauma response (understanding impact)
   - System issues (what the case reveals about systems)

2. **Use the Master Case Summary Table** (`CASES_SUMMARY_TABLE.md`)
   - Find your challenge in the "Index by Key Challenge" section
   - Read the case title and one-sentence lesson
   - Click the Case ID to open the full case

3. **Skim the case**:
   - Read "The Situation" section first (1 minute)
   - Read "Key Challenge" and "Key Lesson" (1 minute)
   - Decide: Is this the right case for your need?

4. **Dive deeper**:
   - Read full case narrative
   - Work through "Reflection Questions" if in training context
   - Apply lesson to your situation

**Total time**: 5-30 minutes depending on depth needed

## Part 1: How to Find the Right Case

### Method 1: Find by Professional Challenge

**Use this if**: You're facing a specific decision or problem right now.

**Steps**:
1. Open `CASES_SUMMARY_TABLE.md`
2. Go to "Index by Key Challenge" section
3. Find your challenge listed (e.g., "Distinguishing abuse from cultural practice")
4. Click the Case ID to open the full case
5. Read the case and apply the lesson to your situation

**Example**:
- You're unsure if something is discipline or abuse → CGRA-001: The Puzzle of Bruises
- You're working with an exploited youth → CGRA-008: Trapped in the City

---

### Method 2: Find by Domain

**Use this if**: You specialize in one area (e.g., trafficking, disability) and want to build expertise.

**Steps**:
1. Open `CASES_SUMMARY_TABLE.md`
2. Go to "Index by Domain" section
3. Find your domain (Maltreatment, Neglect, Exploitation, etc.)
4. Read all related cases
5. Compare and contrast what you learn

**Example Specialization Paths**:
- **Trafficking specialist**: Start with CGRA-008 & CGRA-009
- **Disability rights**: Start with CGRA-013 & CGRA-015
- **Health focus**: Start with CGRA-013 & CGRA-014
- **Family preservation**: Start with CGRA-005, CGRA-006, CGRA-007, CGRA-011

---

### Method 3: Find by Age Group

**Use this if**: You work with a specific age group and want age-appropriate practice wisdom.

**Steps**:
1. Open `CASES_SUMMARY_TABLE.md`
2. Go to "Index by Age Group" section
3. Select the age group (Early Childhood, Middle Childhood, Adolescence)
4. Read all cases for that group
5. Notice patterns in how challenges differ by age

**Why this matters**:
- Early childhood (5-7): High dependency, limited communication
- Middle childhood (8-11): School-age awareness, developing independence
- Adolescence (12-15): Identity formation, risk-taking, disclosure readiness

---

### Method 4: Find by Your Context

**Use this if**: You want to see how cases are handled in your region/country.

**Steps**:
1. Open `CASES_SUMMARY_TABLE.md`
2. Go to "Index by Geographic Region" section
3. Read cases from your region
4. Also read cases from similar contexts
5. Notice what's universal and what's culturally specific

**Why this matters**:
- Different countries have different legal frameworks
- Cultural norms around discipline, family structure, help-seeking vary
- Resources available differ dramatically
- Learning from cases in your region builds cultural competence

---

### Method 5: Find by Complexity Level

**Use this if**: You're learning progressively and want to match case difficulty to your skill level.

**Steps**:
1. Open `CASES_SUMMARY_TABLE.md`
2. Go to "Case Characteristics at a Glance" → "By Trainer Skill Level"
3. Start with "Accessible (new trainers)" cases
4. Move to "Intermediate" after completing foundations
5. Tackle "Advanced" cases after building expertise

**Progression Model**:
- **Week 1-2**: 2-3 accessible cases (kinship, poverty, disclosure)
- **Week 3-4**: 3-4 intermediate cases (mixed challenges)
- **Week 5+**: Advanced cases with nuance and ambiguity

## Part 2: How to Read & Learn from a Case

### Reading Structure

Each case is organized this way:

```
Case ID & Title
├─ THE SITUATION (what happened)
├─ KEY CHALLENGE (the professional dilemma)
├─ KEY LESSON (what this teaches)
├─ DEEPER CONTEXT (why this matters)
├─ REFLECTION QUESTIONS (for learning)
├─ PRACTITIONER WISDOM (what experts say)
├─ RELATED CASES (where to learn more)
└─ RESOURCES (for deeper study)
```

### Reading Strategies by Your Needs

#### Strategy 1: Quick Reference (5 minutes)
**Use when**: You need quick insight for a decision right now

**Read**:
1. THE SITUATION (skim for key facts)
2. KEY CHALLENGE (what's really being asked?)
3. KEY LESSON (what's the takeaway?)

**Skip**: Deeper context, reflection questions, resources

---

#### Strategy 2: Learning Depth (20-30 minutes)
**Use when**: You have time to genuinely understand the case

**Read**:
1. THE SITUATION (full narrative)
2. DEEPER CONTEXT (why this challenge matters)
3. KEY CHALLENGE & LESSON (in full)
4. Work through REFLECTION QUESTIONS
5. Scan PRACTITIONER WISDOM (expert perspectives)

**Output**: One-page summary of learning

---

#### Strategy 3: Critical Thinking (45-60 minutes)
**Use when**: You want to deeply challenge assumptions

**Read**:
1. THE SITUATION (twice, noting your reactions)
2. Work through REFLECTION QUESTIONS in detail
3. PRACTITIONER WISDOM (read all expert perspectives)
4. DEEPER CONTEXT (understand systems at play)
5. KEY CHALLENGE & LESSON (in light of everything above)
6. RELATED CASES (compare and contrast)

**Output**: Written analysis of your learning and where you disagreed

---

#### Strategy 4: Facilitation Prep (60+ minutes)
**Use when**: You're training others using this case

**Read**:
1. Everything (full case)
2. Training Notes (specific guidance for your group)
3. Develop 3-5 discussion prompts (beyond reflection questions)
4. Plan 1-2 role plays or activities
5. Identify potential discussion challenges (e.g., "Someone will blame the family")
6. Prepare counter-frames (e.g., "Let's notice what the family was managing")

**Output**: Trainer guide tailored to your audience

---

### Reflection Questions Framework

Each case includes reflection questions organized by depth level:

**Level 1 - Understanding (What happened?)**
- Basic comprehension of the case situation
- What were the key events?
- Who made what decisions?

**Level 2 - Analysis (Why did it happen?)**
- Understanding causes and context
- What led to this situation?
- What options did the professional have?

**Level 3 - Application (How would I handle this?)**
- Applying to your own practice
- What would you have done differently?
- What's one thing you'd do the same?

**Level 4 - Integration (What does this mean for my practice?)**
- Connecting to values and identity
- What does this case teach about your role?
- How will you practice differently?

**Pro Tip**: Work through levels in order. Level 4 is much harder if you skip Level 1.

## Part 3: How to Apply Cases to Similar Situations

### Case-to-Practice Bridge

When you see a situation similar to a case, use this process:

#### Step 1: Identify the Similarity
```
The case involved:          Your situation involves:
├─ Physical injury          ├─ Bruising on child
├─ Cultural difference      ├─ Family from different culture
└─ Assessment challenge     └─ Not sure if abuse or practice

Similarity: Same basic assessment challenge
```

#### Step 2: Extract the Principle (not the specific answer)
```
CGRA-001 teaches:
├─ DON'T assume maltreatment without exploration
├─ DO ask about cultural context and history
├─ DO involve cultural consultants
└─ PRINCIPLE: Assessment requires humility and questioning

(Not: "This specific family is fine" — every case is different)
```

#### Step 3: Adapt to Your Context
```
Case: Guatemala, economic poverty
Your situation: Urban USA, different poverty

Apply PRINCIPLE:
├─ Ask family about their practices
├─ Get cultural perspective (if different culture)
├─ Get medical opinion on injury
├─ Check history with this family
├─ Document your reasoning

Same principle, different execution
```

#### Step 4: Stay Alert to Differences
```
Case factors:           Your situation factors:
├─ Age 7              ├─ Age 4 (more vulnerable)
├─ Articulate         ├─ Non-verbal (harder to understand)
├─ One child          ├─ Siblings (repeated pattern concern)
└─ First report       └─ Repeated reports (escalates concern)

More factors for concern → May need different response than case
```

### Common Application Scenarios

#### Scenario A: You're in Assessment
**Case to use**: CGRA-001, CGRA-013 (assessment challenges)

**Apply by**:
1. Notice what in the case is similar to your situation
2. Notice what your situation adds (complications)
3. Use the case's questioning process, not the case's answer
4. Ask the same exploratory questions
5. Get expert consultation (same as case did)

**What NOT to do**: "Oh, this is like CGRA-001, so it's definitely not abuse."

**What TO do**: "This is like CGRA-001. I need to explore cultural context, get medical evaluation, and think about history. Let me proceed deliberately."

---

#### Scenario B: You're Engaging a Family or Youth
**Case to use**: CGRA-003, CGRA-008, CGRA-009 (engagement cases)

**Apply by**:
1. Notice what made engagement work in the case
2. Notice barriers that the practitioner overcame
3. Identify what the child/family needed to trust the worker
4. Offer those conditions in your work
5. Monitor whether trust is building

**What NOT to do**: Copy the exact words or approach from the case.

**What TO do**: "The case shows that trust came from (1) consistency, (2) believing the youth, (3) moving at their pace. How can I provide these?"

---

#### Scenario C: You're Making a Big Decision
**Case to use**: CGRA-005, CGRA-006, CGRA-007, CGRA-011, CGRA-012 (decision cases)

**Apply by**:
1. Notice the decision the case presents
2. Read how the practitioner thought about it
3. Notice what information they gathered
4. Notice what they weighted heavily and what they didn't
5. Gather similar information and think similarly

**Reflection for each decision case**:
- What did they prioritize? (child safety? family preservation? connection?)
- Do you agree with their prioritization?
- Would you weight factors differently?
- Why?

---

### Cautions: When NOT to Apply a Case

**Don't use a case for:**
1. **Specific legal templates** (law differs by region; get legal advice)
2. **Diagnosis or medical conclusions** (consult medical experts)
3. **Exact scripting** (your words need your authenticity)
4. **Avoiding professional consultation** ("The case did X, so I don't need to ask")
5. **Blaming outcomes** ("The case shows this doesn't work, so why try?")

**DO use cases for**:
- Understanding decision-making processes
- Learning what questions to ask
- Recognizing patterns and possibilities
- Building confidence in your own thinking
- Sparking discussion about better practice

## Part 4: Using Cases for Staff Training

### Designing a Training Module Around Cases

#### Module 1: Assessment & Cultural Humility (2 hours)
**Audience**: New assessors, supervisors
**Cases**: CGRA-001, CGRA-013, CGRA-004

**Structure**:
1. **Opening** (5 min): Poll - "Have you ever missed something because you assumed?"
2. **Case 1: CGRA-001** (20 min): Read together, discuss assessment process
3. **Case 2: CGRA-013** (20 min): Read together, discuss misidentification
4. **Case 3: CGRA-004** (15 min): Read, discuss subtle signs
5. **Application** (20 min): Bring your own case, apply framework
6. **Closing** (10 min): Commitments - "One thing I'll do differently"

---

#### Module 2: Engagement & Disclosure (2 hours)
**Audience**: Caseworkers, advocates, counselors
**Cases**: CGRA-003, CGRA-008, CGRA-009

**Structure**:
1. **Opening** (10 min): Why disclosure matters; barriers to disclosure
2. **Case 1: CGRA-003** (25 min): Disclosure after years of silence
3. **Case 2: CGRA-008** (25 min): Building trust with exploited youth
4. **Case 3: CGRA-009** (15 min): Early recognition of grooming
5. **Skill Practice** (20 min): Role plays with each case scenario
6. **Closing** (5 min): Key takeaways for your practice

---

#### Module 3: Family Preservation Decisions (2.5 hours)
**Audience**: Supervisors, case reviewers, kinship coordinators
**Cases**: CGRA-005, CGRA-006, CGRA-007, CGRA-011

**Structure**:
1. **Opening** (10 min): Family preservation framework; safety vs. stability
2. **Case 1: CGRA-005** (25 min): Kinship care decision-making
3. **Case 2: CGRA-006** (25 min): Supporting families in poverty
4. **Case 3: CGRA-007** (20 min): Parental incapacity assessment
5. **Case 4: CGRA-011** (20 min): Substance use and parental recovery
6. **Decision Workshop** (20 min): Apply framework to real case
7. **Closing** (10 min): Policy implications and advocacy

#### Module 4: Trauma-Informed Practice (2.5 hours)
**Audience**: All child-serving professionals
**Cases**: CGRA-002, CGRA-010, CGRA-012, CGRA-014

**Structure**:
1. **Opening** (10 min): Neurobiology of trauma; impact on behavior

4. **Case 3: CGRA-012** (20 min): Separation and attachment

### Training Methods Using Cases

#### Method 1: Case Conference
**Best for**: Intermediate-advanced learners, small groups (8-12)
**Time**: 60-90 minutes per case

**Process**:
1. Everyone reads case before meeting (assigned homework)
2. Trainer opens with 1-2 questions
3. Group discusses: "What would you do?"
4. Trainer doesn't announce "right answer" — facilitates thinking
5. Group builds toward shared understanding
6. Trainer offers expert perspective (but group perspective matters)

**What makes it work**:
- Everyone prepared (if not, do case read-aloud instead)
- Facilitator genuinely curious, not just waiting for "right" answer
- Time for real thinking (not rushing)
- Space for disagreement

---

#### Method 2: Structured Case Study
**Best for**: Large groups, online learning, self-paced
**Time**: 30-45 minutes per case

**Process**:
1. Case presentation (trainer reads aloud or video)
2. Guided reflection questions (small groups or individual)
3. Structured discussion (pairs, triads, large group)
4. Expert commentary (trainer synthesis)
5. Application to practice
6. Closing reflection

**What makes it work**:
- Clear structure keeps participants on track
- Questions move from comprehension → application
- Large group doesn't prevent thoughtful engagement

---

#### Method 3: Role Play & Scenario
**Best for**: Interactive learning, skill-building, kinesthetic learners
**Time**: 45-60 minutes per case

**Process**:
1. Set the scene (describe situation at a specific decision point)
2. Assign roles (caseworker, parent, supervisor, observer)
3. Role play the interaction (5-10 minutes)
4. Pause and debrief (what just happened?)
5. Discuss real case (how did it compare?)
6. Do alternate scenario (what if...)

**What makes it work**:
- Moves beyond intellectual understanding to embodied learning
- Participants feel what it's actually like
- Safe space to try, fail, learn, revise

---

#### Method 4: Comparative Case Analysis
**Best for**: Critical thinking, advanced learners, curriculum design
**Time**: 60-90 minutes for 2-3 cases

**Process**:
1. Read two related cases (e.g., CGRA-005 & CGRA-007)
2. Compare & contrast (what's similar? different?)
3. Identify principles (what's universal? what's context-specific?)
4. Debate (which approach is better? for whom? why?)
5. Synthesize learning (what do you believe now?)

**What makes it work**:
- Forces deeper thinking than single case
- Builds nuance ("both/and" not "either/or")
- Engages people who like debate and critical analysis

---

### Training Design Checklist

Before you train using a case:

```
Preparation
☐ Read the full case thoroughly
☐ Work through reflection questions yourself
☐ Identify 2-3 discussion questions unique to your group
☐ Anticipate where people might get stuck or defensive
☐ Plan how you'll handle disagreement respectfully
☐ Gather materials (printed cases, handouts, etc.)

Facilitation
☐ Start with case-neutral opening (experience, values, beliefs)
☐ Present case clearly (read aloud helps)
☐ Ask open questions first ("What do you notice?")
☐ Before giving "expert answer," hear all voices
☐ Validate perspectives even if you disagree
☐ Connect to learners' real practice

Closing
☐ Check for understanding (quick poll or reflection)
☐ Bridge to application (how will you use this?)
☐ Make space for questions or disagreement
☐ Thank people for engagement (learning is work)
```

## Part 5: How to Create Training Modules from Cases

### Step-by-Step: From Case to Course

#### Step 1: Define Your Learning Outcome
```
What should people be able to DO after this training?

Examples:
- "Assess bruising without cultural bias"
- "Engage an exploited youth"
- "Make family preservation decisions"
- "Recognize signs of emotional abuse"
- "Understand trafficking dynamics"

One outcome per module = focused learning
```

#### Step 2: Select 2-4 Cases That Teach It
```
For outcome: "Assess bruising without cultural bias"

Select:
- CGRA-001 (physical injury + cultural difference)
- CGRA-013 (disability misdiagnosed as abuse)
- Consider: CGRA-004 (emotional abuse for comparison)

NOT: Every case about assessment (too much)
NOT: Only one case (not enough diversity)
SWEET SPOT: 2-4 cases, building in complexity
```

#### Step 3: Organize Cases for Learning Progression
```
Case Order Matters:

Option A - Progression from Simple to Complex:
1. CGRA-005 (clearest kinship decision)
2. CGRA-006 (adds poverty complexity)
3. CGRA-007 (adds parental incapacity)
4. CGRA-011 (adds substance use + recovery)

Option B - Progression from Theory to Application:
1. CGRA-001 (introduces framework: cultural humility)
2. CGRA-013 (applies framework: common mistake)
3. CGRA-004 (applies to subtle signs: emotional abuse)

Choose the progression that makes sense for your content
```

#### Step 4: Design Learning Activities
```
For each case, plan 3 activities:

Activity 1 (Comprehension)
- What happened in the case?
- What options did the professional have?
- What did they decide? Why?

Activity 2 (Analysis)
- What assumptions was the professional making?
- Where could bias enter?
- What would you have done?

Activity 3 (Application)
- You see something similar in your practice
- How would you approach it?
- What questions would you ask?
```

#### Step 5: Build Support Materials
```
For trainers:
- Trainer guide (2 pages): case overview + facilitation notes
- Discussion prompt cards (3-5 per case)
- Role play scripts (if applicable)
- Video introduction (optional)

For learners:
- Case reading (full case or summary)
- Reflection questions handout
- One-page case snapshot (key facts)
- Practice application worksheet

For supervisors/reviewers:
- Learning outcomes map
- Assessment rubric (how do we know they learned?)
- Feedback guide for role plays
```

#### Step 6: Pilot & Refine
```
First delivery:
- Deliver to small group (8-12 people)
- Collect feedback (what worked? what didn't?)
- Notice where people got stuck
- Note unexpected learning moments
- Revise before broader delivery

Revision focus:
- Cases: Are they generating good discussion?
- Questions: Are they clear? Generative?
- Timing: Too rushed? Too slow?
- Engagement: Who participated? Who was silent? Why?
```

## Part 6: Using Cases at Different Experience Levels

**Your needs**:
- Building basic vocabulary and frameworks
- Developing confidence in your own thinking
- Seeing that real situations are complex
- Learning that mistakes are normal and navigable

**Best cases to start with**:
- CGRA-005: The Grandparent Question (clear kinship question)
- CGRA-006: Hunger & School (poverty vs. neglect)
- CGRA-014: Living with Seizures (family stress, not maltreatment)
- CGRA-003: The Letter That Changed Everything (disclosure)

**How to use them**:
- Read with a mentor or supervisor
- Work through all reflection questions
- Start with "accessible" level cases
- Don't skip the "expert perspective" sections
- Ask questions freely

**Time investment**: 45 minutes per case

---

### For Intermediate Practitioners (1-5 years)
**Your needs**:
- Building nuance and avoiding oversimplification
- Developing judgment in gray-area cases
- Understanding systemic factors
- Learning from mistakes

**Best cases to focus on**:
- CGRA-001: The Puzzle of Bruises (assessment without bias)
- CGRA-007: The Missing Parent (parental capacity)
- CGRA-011: The Overdose in the Next Room (substance use)
- CGRA-010: Shattered & Rebuilding (complex trauma)
- CGRA-004: Invisible Chains (subtle abuse)

**How to use them**:
- Lead small-group discussions
- Bring your own similar cases to compare
- Challenge your own assumptions
- Consider what information you'd want vs. what you had
- Write a page of your own reflection

**Time investment**: 30 minutes per case, plus discussion

---

### For Experienced Practitioners (5+ years)
**Your needs**:
- Deepening wisdom and cultural competence
- Mentoring others

- Addressing your own blind spots

**Best cases to focus on**:
- CGRA-013: The Misdiagnosis (disability rights)

---

### For Supervisors & Managers
**Your needs**:
- Understanding practice patterns in your team
- Identifying training gaps
- Making case-review decisions
- Advocating for resources and policy

**Best cases to focus on**:

**How to use them**:
- Use in supervision (case reviews)

**Time investment**: Case-dependent, but investment pays off in team practice

## Part 7: Tips & Troubleshooting

### Troubleshooting Common Challenges

#### "This case seems too perfect / unrealistic"
**What's happening**: You're noticing how cases are structured for teaching.
**What to remember**: 
- Real situations are messier; cases are meant to highlight key learning
- The "unrealistic" clarity is actually a gift—use it to learn the pattern
- When you see real situations, they'll be more complex, but the core pattern will be there

**What to do**: 
- Study the case as if it's simplified but essentially true
- Note where you'd expect more complexity in real cases
- When you see something similar, add the complexity

---

#### "I disagree with how the worker handled this"
**What's happening**: You're thinking critically. Great.
**What to remember**: 
- You don't have to agree with every case choice
- Disagreeing is valuable if you can articulate why
- Sometimes the "wrong" choice teaches as much as the right one

**What to do**:
- Name specifically what you'd do differently
- Articulate why (values? new knowledge? context?)
- Compare to real cases you've experienced
- Use this to clarify your own values

---

#### "This case doesn't feel real"
**What's happening**: You're sensing either: (a) it's simplified or (b) it's a real case from somewhere you don't work.
**What to remember**: 
- All fictional cases are simplified (by design)
- Real cases, when added, will reflect real contexts
- Cases from other countries/systems may feel unfamiliar but are still valid

**What to do**:
- Identify what feels "off" (too neat? different system? different culture?)
- Use that difference to learn (what would I do in that context?)
- Find cases from your context in the summary table

---

#### "I'm getting nothing out of this case"
**What's happening**: The case might not fit your learning need, or you're not engaging deeply enough.
**What to remember**: 
- Not every case is for you
- Different cases click at different times
- Sometimes you need to read something twice

**What to do**:
- Ask yourself: Does this case address something I actually need to learn?
- If no: Choose a different case using the indexes
- If yes: Try different reading strategy (method 2, 3, or 4 from Part 2)
- If still nothing: Discuss with a colleague or supervisor

---

#### "The case makes me want to blame the family"
**What's happening**: You're reacting to injustice or failure. Normal response.
**What to remember**: 
- Blame doesn't help
- Cases show complexity; people rarely choose harm
- Professional role is to understand, not judge

**What to do**:
- Name the feeling (anger, frustration, sadness)
- Ask: What was this family managing?
- Look for: What was beyond their control?
- Shift: How could the system have supported them better?
- Apply: How can I reduce barriers in my work?

---

#### "I don't have time for full case study"
**What's happening**: You're busy. That's real.
**What to remember**: 
- Quick reference (Method 1 in Part 2) takes 5-10 minutes
- Even brief engagement builds practice wisdom
- Consistency beats perfection

**What to do**:
- Use "Quick Start" section at top of guide
- Read only "THE SITUATION" and "KEY LESSON"
- One case per month > studying one case intensively then nothing
- Build in time (e.g., 10 minutes per team meeting for one case)

### Making Cases Work for Your Team

#### Getting Your Team Engaged
```
Step 1: Start with voluntary opt-in
- Announce case study option
- Don't mandate
- Invite interested people

Step 2: Use one case in team meeting
- Read aloud (10 min)
- 3 reflection questions (15 min)
- Close with "what's one thing you'll do differently?" (5 min)

Step 3: Notice what happens
- Who engages?
- What questions get asked?
- Where does discomfort show up?

Step 4: Adjust based on response
- If positive: expand to more cases
- If skeptical: use cases supervisors know and trust first
- If avoiding: might need to address deeper team dynamics
```

#### Building Case Culture
```
Monthly Case Study (1 hour)
├─ 15 min: People arrive, brief case introduction
├─ 15 min: Read case (aloud or everyone reads)
├─ 20 min: Discussion (facilitator guides with open questions)
├─ 8 min: Application (each person, one thing they'll do different)
└─ 2 min: Closing (name what we learned together)

Quarterly Case Review
└─ Look at team's actual cases
   ├─ Which ones were handled well?
   ├─ Which challenged us?
   ├─ Which CGRA case best relates?
   └─ What can we learn from comparison?

Coaching with Cases
├─ When you're supervising someone
├─ They say: "I don't know how to handle this"
├─ You ask: "Know any CGRA case similar?"
├─ Read it together
├─ Extract principle
└─ Apply to their case
```

## Part 8: Finding & Adding New Cases

### When Real Cases Are Added to the System

As real cases are ethically collected and integrated, the system will include:

#### Where to Find Real Cases
- Real cases will be marked: CGRA-REAL-001, CGRA-REAL-002, etc.
- They'll appear in the Master Summary Table with notation
- New real cases will be announced in team updates

#### Why Real Cases Matter
- Authentic complexity that fictional cases approximate
- Cultural nuance from actual communities
- Longer-term outcomes showing actual trajectories
- Error stories that teach humility
- Success stories showing what's possible

#### Using Real Cases

## Final Thoughts

The cases in this collection exist because someone, somewhere, faced these challenges. A family struggled. A practitioner thought carefully. An outcome emerged—sometimes good, sometimes incomplete.

By studying these cases, you're:
- Learning from others' experience (building on their wisdom)
- Reducing your own trial-and-error (faster learning)
- Developing judgment (case reasoning helps)
- Building humility (every case shows how little we know)
- Connecting to purpose (why we do this work)

You're not copying answers. You're learning how to think.

The best practitioners are case-learners. They notice patterns. They stay curious. They hold complexity. They know when to act and when to wait. They respect families while protecting children. They know rules matter and rules aren't enough.

This case collection is built for that kind of learning.

Start with one case. Read it deeply. Let it change how you think.

Then pick another.

The wisdom you build will carry you through your career.
