# PART 5: BACK MATTER

## GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS

### A

**Abandonment:** The act of a parent or legal guardian voluntarily relinquishing parental rights and responsibilities; used in legal contexts regarding child welfare and SIJS eligibility.

**Absconsion:** The act of running away or leaving without permission, particularly in legal contexts involving detained juveniles or those under court supervision.

**Abuse:** Intentional infliction of physical injury, sexual abuse, or severe emotional/psychological harm on a child.

**Acculturation:** The process of adapting to a new culture; often relevant for immigrant and refugee youth integrating into U.S. systems.

**Adjudication:** In juvenile justice context, the formal determination by a court that a youth committed a delinquent act; equivalent to guilty verdict in adult court.

**Adversarial Proceeding:** A legal proceeding in which two parties present opposing evidence and arguments before a judge or judicial officer.

**Advocate/Advocacy:** Representation and support for a person's rights and interests in systems (legal, educational, medical, social service).

**Aftercare:** Services and support provided to a youth after release from a detention or residential facility; reentry support.

**Aid to Families with Dependent Children (AFDC):** Federal welfare program providing cash assistance to low-income families with children; now replaced by TANF.

**Alignment:** In systems context, coordination and consistency of policies, practices, and goals across agencies and providers.

**Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR):** Methods of resolving disputes outside traditional litigation, including mediation and arbitration.

**Alternative to Detention:** Community-based options to incarceration, including electronic monitoring, day reporting centers, and probation.

**Appropriate Educational Services:** Services that meet a student's unique educational needs as determined by IEP or 504 plan; required by law.

### B

**Bail/Bond:** Money or property pledged to secure release from custody; bail set by court after arrest.

**Behavioral Intervention:** Planned strategies to address problematic behavior, often based on applied behavior analysis principles.

**Best Interests of the Child:** Legal standard used in custody and child welfare decisions; prioritizes child's safety, stability, and wellbeing.

**Beyond Reasonable Doubt:** Highest standard of proof in criminal law; required to convict in adult or juvenile delinquency proceedings.

**Bifurcated Proceeding:** Legal proceeding divided into two phases, such as guilt phase and sentencing phase.

**Biological Parent/Guardian:** Parent related by blood; may differ from legal or custodial parent.

**Bond:** See Bail/Bond.

**Bullying:** Repeated, intentional harm or harassment directed at a person(s), particularly in school settings; increasingly addressed in law.

### C

**Care Coordination:** Service designed to ensure continuity and integration of care across providers and systems; managed by care coordinator.

**Caregiver Burden:** Stress experienced by person(s) responsible for care of dependent individual(s); risk factor for neglect.

**Case Management:** Broad service designed to assess needs, plan services, coordinate providers, and monitor progress.

**Case Plan:** Written document outlining child's needs, goals, services, and family involvement; legally required in child welfare cases.

**Casework:** Relationship-based service involving assessment, planning, intervention, and support provided by caseworker.

**Cause & Contribution:** Evidence sufficient to establish that a parent or caregiver's action or inaction caused or contributed to child's abuse or neglect.

**Certification to Adult Court:** Legal process transferring juvenile case to adult criminal court; used for serious offenses in some states.

**Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Therapeutic approach addressing thoughts, feelings, and behaviors; evidence-based for trauma and mental health conditions.

**Cognitive Development:** Development of thinking, reasoning, problem-solving, and memory capacities; influenced by age, experience, and environment.

**Community-Based Services:** Services and supports provided in community settings rather than institutions; often preferred to residential placement.

**Community Protective Factors:** Family, school, and community strengths and resources that reduce risk and promote resilience.

**Competence:** Legal determination that person has capacity to stand trial, testify, or make decisions; different from guilt/innocence.

**Concurrent Planning:** Simultaneous planning for multiple permanency goals (reunification and alternative permanency) to ensure child achieves permanency.

**Confidentiality:** Legal protection ensuring certain information cannot be disclosed without authorization; often applies to medical, mental health, and juvenile justice records.

**Consent Decree:** Legal agreement in which party acknowledges alleged actions and agrees to specific conditions without admitting guilt.

**Continuous Assessment:** Ongoing evaluation of child's needs, risks, strengths, and progress throughout case.

**Contributing Factor to Abuse/Neglect:** Parental or caregiver action or inaction that contributed to child's abuse or neglect.

**Conviction:** Legal determination of guilt in criminal or delinquency proceeding; requires proof beyond reasonable doubt.

**Co-occurring Disorders:** Presence of multiple mental health, substance use, or developmental disorders in same individual.

**Coping Strategies:** Techniques or behaviors individuals use to manage stress, emotion, or challenging situations.

**Court-Ordered Services:** Services mandated by court as condition of custody, probation, or case closure.

**Criminal Justice System:** System addressing criminal conduct including police, prosecutors, courts, and corrections; separate from juvenile justice system.

**Crisis Intervention:** Immediate response to emergency situation to ensure safety and stabilization; may precede longer-term services.

### D

**Day Treatment Program:** Structured program providing therapeutic and educational services during daytime hours while youth remains in home.

**Defendant:** Person accused of crime in criminal proceeding; may be adult or youth.

**Deferential Standard of Review:** Appellate standard under which lower court decision given significant deference; used for family law decisions.

**Deficit-Focused Approach:** Service approach emphasizing problems and needs; contrasts with strengths-based approach.

**Delinquency:** In juvenile justice, violation of law by person under age of majority; legal determination rather than clinical diagnosis.

**Delinquent Act:** Conduct that would constitute crime if committed by adult.

**Denial of Services:** Practice of refusing to provide services to eligible person(s); often rooted in discrimination or resource constraints.

**Department of Child & Family Services (DCFS):** Government agency responsible for child welfare, child protection, and family services; may use different names in different states.

**Dependency:** Legal status of child determined by court to have been abused, neglected, or abandoned; child becomes ward of state.

**Dependent Child:** Child determined by court to require protection due to abuse, neglect, or parental incapacity.

**Deposition:** Pretrial testimony given under oath, typically recorded by court reporter.

**Developmental Delay:** Significant lag in achieving developmental milestones expected for age; may indicate disability.

**Developmental Disability:** Significant functional limitation originating before age 22 and expected to continue indefinitely; includes intellectual disabilities, cerebral palsy, and autism.

**Developmental Trauma Disorder:** Proposed diagnosis reflecting complex trauma exposure in childhood; exhibits across multiple domains.

**Developmental Appropriateness:** Age and developmentally appropriate expectations, activities, and services.

**Dextral/Sinistral:** Refers to right-handedness/left-handedness; relevant in discussing developmental and neurological status.

**Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT):** Psychotherapy approach combining CBT with dialectical philosophy; evidence-based for self-harm and suicidality.

**Diligent Search:** Active effort to locate absent or missing parent(s) before terminating parental rights; required by law.

**Diligent Efforts:** Reasonable attempts by child welfare agency to prevent removal and maintain family unity.

**Discharge Planning:** Preparation for youth's exit from facility or services; includes safety planning and service continuation.

**Discrepancy Model:** Assessment approach using significant gap between ability and achievement to identify learning disability; now less preferred than comprehensive evaluation.

**Dismissal:** Termination of legal case without judgment; case resolved without proceeding to trial/adjudication.

**Disproportionality:** Overrepresentation of particular groups (by race, ethnicity, class, gender) in system (child welfare, juvenile justice); indicates structural inequity.

**Dispositional Hearing:** In juvenile or family court, hearing to determine appropriate services, placement, or consequences after adjudication of delinquency or abuse/neglect.

**Dissociation:** Disconnection from thoughts, feelings, memories, or sense of identity; symptom of trauma.

**Dispositional Alternative:** Options available to court in sentencing or case plan; may include probation, residential placement, treatment, or other services.

**Disproportionate Impact:** Policy or practice that has unequal effect on different groups, even if applied uniformly.

**Diversion:** Process of redirecting youth away from formal justice system to community-based services; may be pre-arrest or pre-trial.

**Domestic Violence:** Pattern of abusive behavior in intimate relationship involving physical violence, emotional/psychological abuse, sexual abuse, or controlling behavior.

**Documentation:** Written record of assessment findings, decisions, actions, and outcomes.

**Dropout Prevention:** Services and programs designed to help students remain engaged in school and complete education.

**Drug Court:** Specialized court handling cases involving substance use; uses treatment and monitoring rather than incarceration.

**Dual Diagnosis:** Presence of both mental health disorder and substance use disorder; requires integrated treatment.

**Due Process:** Constitutional requirement that government act fairly according to established rules; includes notice, hearing, counsel, and appeal rights.

### E

**Early Intervention:** Services provided before age 3 to address developmental delays or disabilities; federally funded.

**Ecological Approach:** Service approach considering child within context of family, school, community, and culture; systems-based.

**Economic Deprivation:** Significant lack of financial resources necessary for basic needs; poverty.

**Ecosystem:** Interconnected systems and environments affecting child's development (family, school, community, culture).

**Education Advocate:** Professional or trained individual representing student's educational interests in school or special education contexts.

**Educator:** Professional providing instruction and education to children; includes teachers and other specialists.

**Efficacy:** Effectiveness in producing intended result(s).

**Emotional Abuse:** Pattern of behavior causing significant emotional or psychological harm; includes verbal abuse, isolation, rejection, and terrorizing.

**Emotionally Disturbed (ED):** Special education category for students with emotional/behavioral disorders affecting educational performance.

**Emancipation:** Legal process granting youth adult status and rights; releases parents of custody and support obligations.

**Emergency Detention:** Short-term detention pending court hearing, usually in custody following arrest.

**Emergency Protective Order:** Court order issued to protect person from immediate harm; usually short-term.

**Emergency Services:** Crisis intervention services available 24/7 for urgent/emergency situations.

**Emotional Intelligence:** Ability to recognize, understand, and manage emotions in self and others.

**Empowerment:** Process of increasing person's control, autonomy, and agency in their life and circumstances.

**Engaging Reluctant Clients:** Skills and strategies for building relationships with and supporting clients who are initially resistant to services.

**English Language Learner (ELL):** Student whose primary language is not English; may be developing English proficiency.

**Entitlement Programs:** Government benefits programs (Social Security, Medicaid, food stamps) for which individuals who meet criteria are entitled; may differ from needs-based programs.

**Environment:** Physical, social, cultural, and economic context in which child lives and develops.

**Environmental Factors:** Conditions in child's environment affecting development and wellbeing.

**Evaluation:** Comprehensive assessment using multiple methods (observation, testing, interviews) to determine status, needs, or progress.

**Evidentiary Hearing:** Legal hearing in which evidence is presented to determine disputed facts.

**Evidence-Based Practice:** Service approach or intervention proven effective through rigorous research.

**Exceptional Children:** Children with disabilities requiring special education services.

**Exclusionary Discipline:** Removal of student from school (suspension or expulsion); particularly concerning for students with disabilities.

**Exoneration:** Legal determination of innocence; reversal of conviction.

**Expenditure Ceiling:** Limitation on total amount government or organization will spend on services.

**Expert Witness:** Witness with specialized knowledge permitted to offer opinions in legal proceeding.

**Exposure Therapy:** Therapeutic approach involving gradual exposure to trauma-related cues to reduce fear and avoidance.

### F

**Failure to Thrive:** Medical/developmental condition in infants/young children marked by inadequate growth and development; may indicate neglect.

**Family Assessment:** Comprehensive evaluation of family structure, dynamics, strengths, challenges, and needs.

**Family Engagement:** Process of involving families as active partners in planning and services for their children.

**Family Law:** Body of law addressing family relationships, marriage, divorce, custody, child support, and guardianship.

**Family Preservation:** Services and interventions designed to keep families together and prevent removal of child.

**Family Reunification:** Process of preparing child in out-of-home placement to return to family; core child welfare goal.

**Family Stability:** Degree to which family has consistent, reliable, low-conflict, and supportive relationships and living situation.

**Family Strengths:** Positive qualities, resources, and competencies within families.

**Felony:** More serious category of crime; typically results in incarceration > 1 year.

**Field Placement/Field Work:** Supervised practical experience in professional setting; often part of professional training.

**Financial Assistance:** Money provided to individual or family to meet basic needs; includes public benefits and support services.

**Findings of Fact:** Written determination by court based on evidence presented; precedes conclusions of law.

**First-Degree Abuse/Neglect:** Most serious category of abuse or neglect; often requires imminent danger/serious harm standard.

**First Offense:** Person's first criminal or delinquent act; relevant for sentencing/disposition.

**Forensic Interview:** Specialized interview technique with child to investigate allegations of abuse; conducted by trained professional.

**Forensic Psychologist:** Psychologist with training in legal contexts; may conduct evaluations and testify in court.

**Foster Care:** System of temporary out-of-home placement of child with trained, monitored foster family or facility.

**Foster Parent:** Individual who provides temporary care for child in their home; licensed and supervised.

**Foster-to-Adopt Program:** Program allowing foster parents to adopt children in their care; facilitates permanency.

**Frequency:** How often something occurs; relevant for assessing severity of abuse/neglect patterns.

**Full-Sibling:** Brother or sister sharing both biological parents; distinguished from half-sibling or stepsib.

**Functional Assessment:** Evaluation determining how person functions across settings and domains; often based on observation.

**Functional Capacity Evaluation:** Assessment of person's ability to perform activities of daily living and self-care.

**Functional Family Therapy (FFT):** Evidence-based treatment for youth behavioral problems emphasizing family functioning.

**Functional Limitation:** Restriction in ability to perform expected activities for age and role.

**Funding Stream:** Source of money for program or services; may be federal, state, local, or private.

### G

**Gang Affiliation:** Membership or association with gang organization; may be protective or risk factor depending on context.

**Gangs, Gang Violence:** Organized group involved in criminal activity; major risk factor in some communities.

**Gender Identity:** Sense of being male, female, neither, or other gender; may differ from sex assigned at birth.

**Gender Non-Conformity:** Expression not aligned with cultural expectations for one's gender; not disorder but may subject youth to discrimination.

**Generalization:** Transfer of learning or skills from one context to another; goal of treatment and education.

**Genetic Predisposition:** Inherited tendency toward certain conditions or traits.

**Geocaching:** Using GPS technology for hidden treasure hunting; recreational activity.

**Genetic Counseling:** Professional service providing information about genetic conditions and inheritance patterns.

**Gerontological Assessment:** Evaluation of older adult's functioning and needs.

**Gifted & Talented:** Students with demonstrated exceptional ability requiring advanced educational services.

**Gist:** Essential meaning or main point of something.

**Goal-Directed Behavior:** Actions deliberately oriented toward achieving specific objective.

**Graduated Reentry:** Phased process of youth returning to community after detention/placement, with decreasing supervision.

**Grandparent Custody:** Legal arrangement in which grandparent(s) serve as custodial parent(s).

**Grievance Procedure:** Formal process for addressing complaints or disputes within organization or system.

**Grooming:** Predatory behavior involving relationship-building to manipulate child into abuse; also refers to hygiene practices.

**Group Care:** Residential setting providing care to group of children (group home, residential facility).

**Group Home:** Non-institutional residential placement for small number of children with trained staff.

**Group Therapy:** Therapeutic service provided to multiple individuals with shared issues; often therapeutic for adolescents.

**Groupthink:** Tendency of group to suppress dissent and make poor decisions through pressure to conform.

**Guardianship:** Legal arrangement in which person (guardian) assumes responsibility for care, custody, and control of dependent (ward).

**Guardian ad Litem:** Court-appointed guardian representing child's interests in legal proceeding; may be attorney or trained volunteer.

**Guidance Counselor:** School employee providing career, educational, and personal counseling to students.

**Guided Practice:** Learning approach in which teacher/coach provides support while learner practices new skills.

**Guilt:** Feelings of shame or responsibility, often related to perceived wrongdoing.
