# Case Index by Primary Domain

This index organizes all 15 cases by primary domain of intervention—the major functional area where case work occurs. Many cases span multiple domains; this index lists cases by their primary domain while noting secondary domains.

---

## Documents/Asylum Domain

**Cases: 1, 9, and secondary in: 5, 6, 10, 11, 13, 14**

### What Issues Arise in This Domain

#### Identity and Documentation
- **Missing documents**: No birth certificate, passport, travel documents, or identity papers
- **Fraudulent documents**: False documents provided by smugglers or traffickers; confiscated upon discovery
- **Identity verification complexity**: No way to confirm actual identity; conflicting information from child
- **Language and documentation**: Documents in unfamiliar language; child cannot read or understand
- **Lost documents**: Documents destroyed, lost, or intentionally hidden during journey
- **Chain of custody**: How documentation is managed, stored, and transferred

#### Asylum Process
- **Age classification**: Risk of child being classified as adult to avoid protections
- **Vulnerability assessment**: Identifying and documenting protection needs
- **Asylum claim basis**: Persecution grounds (political, religious, ethnic, social group, opinion)
- **Safe country doctrine**: If child passed through another country, may face readmission
- **Procedural fairness**: Adequate interpretation, representation, and due process
- **Interview trauma**: Asylum interviews may be re-traumatizing; need for trauma-informed process
- **Delay and uncertainty**: Long processing periods creating emotional toll and survival risk
- **Appeal process**: If initial claim denied, appeal rights and procedures

#### Guardianship and Legal Status
- **Guardian appointment**: Official legal guardian assignment (tuteur, curator, etc.)
- **Parental responsibility**: When parents' whereabouts/status unknown
- **Legal representation**: Right to attorney; access to legal services
- **Residency status**: Temporary, conditional, or permanent status
- **Work authorization**: Right to employment; employment restrictions
- **Travel documents**: When asylum granted, getting travel documents for family contact

#### Family Reunification (Legal Aspect)
- **Family identification**: Locating family members
- **Family verification**: Confirming relationships
- **Family location tracing**: Using ICRC, consulates, and networks
- **Legal sponsorship**: Family member sponsoring reunification
- **Visa processes**: Immigration procedures for family to reunite
- **Safe return**: Ensuring return to origin country is safe

### Relevant Laws and Conventions

**International**
- **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**: Binding on most countries; Article 3 (best interest), Article 8 (identity), Article 9 (family separation), Article 10 (family reunification)
- **Convention on the Status of Refugees (1951)**: Defines refugee; non-refoulement principle; rights of refugees
- **Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees (1967)**: Extends 1951 Convention
- **UN Guidelines on International Protection**: Age and gender considerations; trauma-informed interpretation
- **UNACCOMPANIED CHILDREN Guidelines (UNHCR)**: Special protections for unaccompanied minors
- **ILO Conventions**: Child labor (C138, C182); trafficking (C29, C105)

**Regional**
- **EU Asylum Qualification Directive**: Age assessment; vulnerability; family separation
- **European Convention on Human Rights**: Right to family life (Article 8)
- **African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child**: Regional protections

**Domestic Examples**
- **France**: Code de l'Action Sociale et des Familles; tutelle system for minors
- **Belgium**: Asylum Law; age assessment procedures
- **Germany**: Asylum Act; family reunification provisions
- **UK**: Immigration, Asylum and Nationality Act; age assessment
- **U.S.**: Immigration and Nationality Act; Trafficking Victims Protection Act; Special Immigrant Juvenile Status

### Key Resources

**UNHCR**
- Refugee status determination
- Family tracing and reunification
- Country conditions information
- International protection guidelines
- Training and capacity building

**ICRC (International Committee of Red Cross)**
- Family tracing and messaging
- Restoring family links
- Central Tracing Agency (in conflict)
- Migration and family separations

**Legal Organizations**
- International Bar Association (pro bono asylum lawyers)
- American Immigration Lawyers Association
- European Legal Network on Asylum
- ECRE (European Council on Refugees and Exiles)
- National refugee legal services (varies by country)

**Government Agencies**
- Immigration/Asylum authorities
- Consulates and embassies
- Border authorities
- Child protection agencies (coordination with immigration)

**NGOs Specializing in Asylum**
- Amnesty International
- Human Rights Watch
- Save the Children (asylum programs)
- IRC (International Rescue Committee)
- HIAS (formerly Hebrew Immigrant Aid Society)

### Intervention Strategies

#### Immediate (Days 1-7)
1. **Vulnerability assessment** by trained social worker; determine protection needs
2. **Assign legal counsel** experienced in asylum law
3. **Identify and document known information** about child (name, age, origin)
4. **Begin family tracing** if any information available (ICRC, consulates)
5. **Establish guardian or temporary caretaker** if no family identified
6. **Interpret child's concerns** in simple, non-threatening way

#### Short-term (Weeks 1-8)
1. **Age verification**: Ensure correct age assessment (not falsely classified as adult)
2. **Document collection**: Obtain any existing documents; replace lost documents
3. **Vulnerability assessment completion**: Full assessment of protection needs
4. **Interview preparation**: Trauma-informed preparation for asylum interview
5. **Family location**: Intensive tracing efforts using all available channels
6. **Interim status**: Obtain temporary protection while awaiting asylum determination

#### Medium-term (Weeks 2-6 months)
1. **Asylum application filing**: Complete written statement of claim with legal counsel
2. **Evidence gathering**: Collect country conditions evidence, medical records, expert opinions
3. **Interview**: Formal asylum interview with interpretation and legal representation
4. **Family identification**: If possible, establish contact with located family
5. **Appeal preparation**: If initial interview negative, prepare appeal challenge
6. **Status-based rights access**: Ensure child can access services based on current status

#### Long-term (Months 3-24)
1. **Decision implementation**: Whether asylum granted, denied, or appeal pending
2. **Family reunification facilitation**: If family located and safe, facilitate reunification process
3. **Integration or return planning**: Depending on asylum outcome
4. **Documentation for travel**: Obtain documents allowing family contact or reunification
5. **Ongoing legal review**: Monitor for changed circumstances; explore additional protections if needed

#### Case Example: Amira (Case 1)
- **Immediate**: Age verification (14, not 18); legal counsel assigned; guardianship established
- **Short-term**: Medical evaluation; documentation collection; language support; family location attempts
- **Medium-term**: Asylum application filed; medical evidence collected (respiratory infection, malnutrition); school enrollment
- **Long-term**: Asylum granted (January 2025); work permit issued; family contact restored (June 2025); ongoing integration

## Health/Medical Domain

**Cases: 1, 3, 7, 15, and secondary in: 2, 5, 6, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14**

### What Issues Arise in This Domain

#### Acute Medical Conditions
- **Life-threatening illness or injury**: Requires immediate intervention; may need hospitalization
- **Infectious disease exposure**: TB, malaria, diarrheal disease, respiratory infections common in migration
- **Malnutrition**: Severe weight loss; signs of starvation; lack of micronutrients
- **Untreated respiratory infection**: Persistent cough; breathing difficulty; possible TB
- **Physical injuries**: From violence, torture, trafficking, or journey conditions
- **Acute health crisis**: Sudden medical emergency; need for emergency services

#### Chronic Health Conditions
- **Asthma**: Common; exacerbated by stress and environmental triggers
- **Diabetes**: Type 1 or 2; requires consistent medication and monitoring
- **Hypertension**: May develop after trauma; genetic factors
- **Disability**: Physical or sensory disability affecting communication or mobility
- **Ongoing medication needs**: For conditions developed before or during journey
- **Surgical needs**: Procedures deferred due to migration

#### Medical Trauma
- **Negative healthcare experiences**: Mistreatment, pain, or fear during prior medical care
- **Procedural anxiety**: Fear of medical procedures, needles, or examination
- **Trust barriers**: Reluctance to engage with healthcare providers
- **Communication barriers**: Cannot describe symptoms; fear of medical environment
- **Re-traumatization risk**: Medical procedures may trigger trauma memories

#### Public Health/Preventive Issues
- **Immunization gaps**: Missed routine vaccines; no vaccine records
- **Sexual health**: Reproductive health for adolescents; contraception if needed; STI prevention
- **Hygiene and sanitation**: Living in conditions conducive to illness
- **Food security**: Adequate, safe nutrition for growth and health
- **Water and sanitation**: Access to clean water; proper toileting facilities

#### Mental Health Integration with Medical
- **Psychosomatic presentations**: Physical symptoms from psychological stress
- **Sleep problems**: Nightmares, insomnia affecting health
- **Pain complaints**: Without clear medical cause; common in trauma survivors
- **Medication compliance**: Child refusing or unable to take prescribed medications
- **Health behavior**: Eating, exercise, substance use affected by trauma

### Relevant Laws and Guidelines

**International**
- **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**: Article 24 (health and health services); Article 23 (disability)
- **Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities**: Accessibility and inclusion
- **World Health Organization (WHO)**: Guidelines on child health in refugee/migrant populations
- **Standards of Care for Refugees**: Medical standards across humanitarian organizations

**Regional**
- **European Directives**: Reception conditions standards for asylum seekers (including health)
- **African Charter on the Health and Welfare of the Child**: Health rights
- **PAHO (Pan American Health Organization)**: Standards for health in migration

**National Examples**
- **France**: Healthcare access for undocumented children; tuberculosis and vaccination mandates
- **Germany**: Health screening on arrival; mental health services for trauma
- **Canada**: Medical eligibility for asylum claimants; TB screening requirements
- **Australia**: Health assessment requirements; vaccination schedule

**Professional Standards**
- **AAP (American Academy of Pediatrics)**: Guidelines on refugee child health; trauma-informed care
- **AMCP (American College of Physicians)**: Immigrant health guidelines
- **International Society for Pediatric Oncology**: Standards for cancer care in resource-limited settings
- **Trauma-Informed Care Standards**: SAMHSA guidelines; emphasis on safety and trust

### Key Resources

**Medical Providers**
- Pediatricians with refugee/migrant experience
- Family medicine physicians
- Emergency medicine specialists
- Mental health providers (psychiatrists, psychologists)
- Nurses and nurse practitioners
- Physician assistants

**Public Health**
- Local health departments; TB and communicable disease programs
- Vaccination clinics; immunization records access
- Sexual health clinics
- Mental health/behavioral health clinics
- Disability services and rehabilitation

**Specialized Services**
- Refugee health screening programs
- Trauma-informed care centers
- Pediatric mental health specialists
- Developmental pediatricians (for disability)
- Nutritionists/dietitians
- Physical/occupational therapists

**Organizations**
- **IRC (International Rescue Committee)**: Health programs in refugee resettlement
- **MSF (Médecins Sans Frontières/Doctors Without Borders)**: Medical care in crisis
- **UNHCR Health Unit**: Standards and guidance
- **Regional refugee health coalitions**
- **University medical centers**: Training and expertise
- **Telemedicine platforms**: Remote specialist consultation

### Intervention Strategies

#### Immediate (Days 1-7)
1. **Medical triage**: Assess for life-threatening conditions; emergency care if needed
2. **Acute treatment**: Pain management; infection treatment; nutritional support
3. **Safety assessment**: Physical examination for signs of abuse, torture, trafficking
4. **Immunization screening**: TB skin test; vaccination status assessment
5. **Medication reconciliation**: Identify any medications child is taking; assess adequacy
6. **Language and interpretation**: Ensure clear communication; obtain medical history from child

#### Short-term (Weeks 1-8)
1. **Comprehensive medical evaluation**: History, physical exam, appropriate testing
2. **Infectious disease screening**: TB, malaria, hepatitis, STIs; treat positive findings
3. **Nutritional assessment and support**: Weight gain plan; micronutrient supplementation
4. **Immunization plan**: Catch-up vaccinations on appropriate schedule
5. **Medication initiation**: For identified conditions (asthma, diabetes, pain, sleep, etc.)
6. **Trauma screening**: Assess for medical trauma; plan trauma-informed approach

#### Medium-term (Weeks 2-6 months)
1. **Ongoing medication management**: Monitor effectiveness; adjust dosages; refill prescriptions
2. **Specialist referral**: If complex conditions identified (cardiac, endocrine, orthopedic, etc.)
3. **Nutritional rehabilitation**: Continue weight gain; adequate diet; micronutrient replacement
4. **Mental health integration**: Address psychosomatic symptoms; trauma treatment as applicable
5. **Sexual health (adolescents)**: Contraception if needed; STI prevention; reproductive health education
6. **School health coordination**: Share relevant information with school nurse; accommodate health needs

#### Long-term (Months 3-24)
1. **Chronic disease management**: Ongoing monitoring and treatment; self-management skills
2. **Preventive care**: Annual exams; dental care; vision; hearing screening
3. **Developmental follow-up**: Growth monitoring; developmental assessment; early intervention if needed
4. **Mental health continuity**: Ongoing therapy; medication review
5. **Health literacy**: Education for child and caregiver on health maintenance and disease prevention
6. **Transition planning**: As child ages; preparing for adult healthcare

#### Case Example: Karim (Case 3)
- **Immediate**: Emergency care for injuries; trauma screening; medical assessment
- **Short-term**: Nutritional rehabilitation; TB and infectious disease screening; wound care; sleep medication trial
- **Medium-term**: Continued nutrition support; trauma-focused therapy; medical follow-up
- **Long-term**: Ongoing pediatric care; mental health treatment; family reunification with health plans

## Finances/Banking/Economic Domain

**Cases: 5, and secondary in: 1, 2, 8, 10, 12, 14**

### What Issues Arise in This Domain

#### Individual Financial Situation
- **No money**: Child arrives with no funds; needs for immediate survival (food, shelter)
- **No identification**: Cannot open bank account, get work authorization, access benefits
- **No credit history**: Cannot borrow; cannot establish financial identity
- **Remittance needs**: Family may be depending on child's economic support
- **Income sources unclear**: Child may be involved in illegal economy to survive

#### Family Economic Security
- **Family financial dependence**: Family depending on child's labor/income (child labor driver)
- **Debt bondage**: Family owes money; child's labor "paying off" family debt
- **Survival poverty**: Family cannot meet basic needs without child's work or income
- **Economic pressure**: Family pressure to child to earn money, send remittances
- **Lack of sustainable income**: Adult caregiver underemployed or unemployed

#### System Access
- **Benefit eligibility**: Undocumented status limiting access to government assistance
- **Asylum seeker allowance**: Many countries provide minimal allowance for asylum seekers
- **Employment restrictions**: Legal work authorization limited by immigration status
- **Social security numbers**: Often required for employment; undocumented children cannot obtain
- **Tax and withholding**: Issues if child is working illegally; no record or protections

#### Exploitation and Trafficking Risks
- **Trafficker debt**: Trafficker claims child owes debt for transportation/debt bondage
- **Wage theft**: Employer not paying promised wages
- **Unsafe work**: Working without safety equipment; hazardous conditions
- **Forced commercial sexual exploitation**: Child engaged in sex work (often described as "survival sex")
- **Labor trafficking**: Exploitative conditions; inability to leave

#### Assets and Valuables
- **Lost possessions**: Money, jewelry, documents lost or stolen during journey
- **Valuables held by others**: Possessions held by smuggler, trafficker, or facilitator
- **Inheritance or family property**: May have assets in origin country; unable to access

### Relevant Laws and Conventions

**International**
- **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**: Article 32 (protection from economic exploitation)
- **ILO Convention 138 (Minimum Age Convention)**: Minimum working age (generally 15, 18 for hazardous work)
- **ILO Convention 182 (Worst Forms of Child Labor)**: Prohibits hazardous child labor; trafficking; sexual exploitation
- **Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons (UN)**: Definition of trafficking; victim protections
- **Convention on Forced Labour (ILO C29)**: Prohibits forced labor; protections for children
- **Convention on the Rights of Migrant Workers and Their Families**: Economic rights of migrant children

**Regional**
- **EU Directive on Trafficking**: Victim identification and protection; financial compensation
- **African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child**: Economic rights; protection from exploitation

**National Examples**
- **France**: Minimum wage laws; child labor prohibitions; trafficking victim support
- **Belgium**: Benefit system for asylum seekers; age-based work restrictions
- **Germany**: Asylum seeker benefits; work authorization timeline; trafficking victim support
- **UK**: Modern Slavery Act; victim compensation; work restrictions for asylum seekers
- **U.S.**: Fair Labor Standards Act (child labor); Trafficking Victims Protection Act; benefits for trafficking victims

**Professional Standards**
- **ILO Guidelines**: Identification and support for trafficking victims and exploited children
- **UNODC Handbook**: Identification and support of child trafficking victims
- **Red Cross Standards**: Economic support in humanitarian crises

### Key Resources

**Employment and Economic Support**
- Labor departments and wage and hour agencies
- Job training and employment programs
- Microfinance and lending for small business
- Youth employment programs (subsidized or supported employment)
- Educational programs with stipends
- Vocational training programs

**Benefit Programs**
- Social services and public assistance (where eligible)
- Asylum seeker benefits (varies by country)
- Emergency assistance programs
- Food assistance (food stamps, food banks, food programs)
- Housing assistance (shelter, rent support)
- Child care subsidies or support

**Financial Services**
- Banks and credit unions (barriers to access for undocumented)
- Prepaid debit cards (alternative to banking)
- Community development financial institutions
- Money transfer services (for remittances)
- Financial literacy programs

**Specialized Services for Trafficking/Exploitation**
- Trafficking victim services (legal support, emergency assistance, housing)
- Anti-trafficking organizations
- Labor rights organizations and unions
- Domestic worker organizations
- Sexual exploitation support services

**Organizations**
- **International Labour Organization (ILO)**: Child labor elimination; trafficking prevention
- **UNODC (United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime)**: Trafficking victim support
- **ECPAT International**: Commercial sexual exploitation prevention
- **Polaris Project**: Trafficking hotlines and victim support
- **Human Rights Watch**: Child labor monitoring and advocacy
- **Save the Children**: Child protection and economic support programs

### Intervention Strategies

#### Immediate (Days 1-7)
1. **Safety assessment**: Determine if child in exploitative situation; ensure immediate safety
2. **Basic needs provision**: Provide shelter, food, hygiene, and immediate financial support
3. **No coercion**: Do not require repayment or "earn back" support
4. **Family assessment**: If family in poverty, address family situation (not just child)
5. **Document any exploitation**: Document conditions, wages, debts, control mechanisms

#### Short-term (Weeks 1-8)
1. **Survivor support services**: If trafficking/exploitation victim, connect to specialized services
2. **Debt documentation**: If debt bondage claimed, investigate and document illegitimate debts
3. **Family economic assessment**: Assess whether child work or income is family survival strategy
4. **Education access**: Enroll child in school; remove barrier to education
5. **Health and safety**: Ensure child's health needs met; no return to hazardous work
6. **Legal status clarity**: Work toward resolution of immigration status if possible

#### Medium-term (Weeks 2-6 months)
1. **Family economic strengthening**: If family poverty driver, provide economic support
   - Income-generating activity training for parent/guardian
   - Microfinance or small business support
   - Job training or employment support for adults
   - Social safety net enrollment (if eligible)
2. **Education continuation**: Support academic progress; prevent school exit
3. **Skills development**: Vocational skills toward age-appropriate employment when legal
4. **Case monitoring**: Ensure child not returning to exploitative work; address ongoing pressure
5. **Legal protection**: Pursue trafficking victim status if applicable; ensure labor protections

#### Long-term (Months 3-24)
1. **Sustainable livelihood**: Family economic stability reducing exploitation drivers
2. **Age-appropriate employment pathways**: Legitimate work options when legal age/status reached
3. **Education completion**: Support high school completion; vocational/higher education planning
4. **Financial literacy**: Teach money management; prevent future exploitation
5. **Ongoing monitoring**: Track for re-exploitation; continue protective oversight
6. **Legal status resolution**: Work toward permanent status if applicable

#### Case Example: Child Labor Case (Case 5)
- **Immediate**: Removal from hazardous work; safety assessment; documentation
- **Short-term**: Family economic assessment; enrollment in school; health screening
- **Medium-term**: Parent job training; family economic strengthening; child education support
- **Long-term**: Family economic stability; child education completion; legal work when age-appropriate

## Work/Employment Domain

**Cases: 5, 12, and secondary in: 1, 8, 10**

### What Issues Arise in This Domain

#### Employment Authorization
- **Work permit requirements**: Legal authorization to work varies by status
- **Timeline for work permit**: When can child legally work based on immigration status
- **Age and hour restrictions**: Limits on working hours, types of work by age
- **Documentation**: Proof of identity, age, and authorization to work
- **Employer verification**: Whether employer checks legal status
- **Informal economy**: Work in informal sector (domestic work, street vending, etc.) without authorization

#### Education vs. Work Balance
- **School enrollment prioritization**: Education should take precedence over work
- **Part-time work and school**: Balancing education with work for income
- **Work-study programs**: Combining education with supported work experience
- **School exit risk**: Work demands causing school dropout
- **Skill development**: Education and vocational training as pathway to legitimate employment

#### Employer Practices
- **Wage payment**: Employer paying promised wages; no wage theft
- **Working conditions**: Safe, non-hazardous environment; appropriate hours
- **Labor standards compliance**: Employer following minimum wage, hour, and safety laws
- **Contract clarity**: Written or verbal agreement about terms; not changed unilaterally
- **Rights awareness**: Child understanding their labor rights
- **Grievance mechanisms**: Way to report problems and seek resolution

#### Economic Motivation
- **Family income necessity**: Economic desperation driving work decision
- **Autonomous income desire**: Adolescent wanting independence and income
- **Remittance pressure**: Expectations to send money to family
- **Debt**: Owing money; work seen as way to repay
- **Consumption goals**: Wanting money for goods, peer status, etc.

#### Transition to Adulthood
- **Youth employment programs**: Supported employment for older adolescents
- **Career pathways**: Planning for career development, not just short-term work
- **Apprenticeships**: Structured training in trades or skills
- **Ongoing education**: Combining education with employment; not mutually exclusive
- **Future planning**: Work as part of broader life planning, not just survival

### Relevant Laws and Conventions

**International**
- **ILO Convention 138 (Minimum Age Convention)**: Minimum age 15 (14 in developing countries) for work; 18 for hazardous
- **ILO Convention 182 (Worst Forms of Child Labor)**: Hazardous work; trafficking; commercial sexual exploitation; bonded labor
- **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**: Article 32 (protection from economic exploitation); Article 36 (protection from other forms of exploitation)
- **ILO Convention 29 and 105**: Forced labor prohibitions

**Regional**
- **EU Directives**: Young workers protection; working hours; hazardous work restrictions
- **African Charter**: Child labor prohibitions and protection

**National Examples**
- **France**: Minimum working age 15; restricted hours and conditions for minors; no hazardous work
- **Belgium**: Minimum age 15; 15-18 age group restrictions; hazardous work prohibited
- **Germany**: Minimum age 15; restricted hours; apprenticeship standards
- **UK**: Minimum age 13 (light work); 15 for regular work; 18 for hazardous work
- **U.S.**: Fair Labor Standards Act; minimum age 14 (16 for hazardous); hour restrictions for school-age

**Occupational Safety**
- **OSHA (U.S.) Standards**: Hazardous work prohibitions for youth
- **National occupational safety standards**: Varies by country

### Key Resources

**Employment Services**
- Labor departments and employment offices
- Youth employment programs
- Job training and vocational programs
- Apprenticeship programs and coordinators
- Work-study and internship programs
- Supported employment services (for youth with barriers)

**Education and Training**
- Vocational schools and technical colleges
- Community colleges
- Trade unions (apprenticeship training)
- Youth training organizations
- Computer and digital skills programs

**Labor Rights Organizations**
- Labor unions
- Worker advocacy organizations
- Immigrant worker organizations
- Domestic worker organizations
- Street children/working children organizations

**Oversight and Protection**
- Labor department inspectorates
- Occupational safety authorities
- Anti-trafficking organizations (for exploitative work)
- Child protection services
- Legal aid for labor disputes

**Organizations**
- **International Labour Organization (ILO)**: Standards, guidance, and programs
- **UNODC**: Trafficking and forced labor prevention
- **Save the Children**: Child labor elimination programs
- **ECPAT International**: Commercial exploitation prevention
- **Various anti-trafficking and worker rights organizations**

### Intervention Strategies

#### Immediate (Days 1-7)
1. **Assess work situation**: What work is child doing? Who is employer? What are conditions and pay?
2. **Safety assessment**: Is work hazardous or exploitative? Is child safe?
3. **Document concerns**: Record conditions, wages, hours, any abuse or control
4. **No immediate work pressure**: Focus on stabilization and assessment, not work placement

#### Short-term (Weeks 1-8)
1. **Education enrollment**: Prioritize school enrollment; discuss education-work balance
2. **Health assessment**: Ensure no health impacts from current or prior work
3. **Rights education**: Teach child about labor rights in accessible way
4. **Family economic assessment**: If family driving work, address family situation
5. **Legal status clarity**: Understand what work authorization is available or will be available

#### Medium-term (Weeks 2-6 months)
1. **Career planning**: Discuss long-term goals; education as foundation
2. **Skill development**: Focus on education and skill-building
3. **Transition planning**: If in hazardous work, plan transition to safe work or school-only
4. **Part-time work if appropriate**: If older adolescent and stable, explore part-time safe work combined with school
5. **Economic stabilization**: Ensure family not pushing into work; address family needs

#### Long-term (Months 3-24)
1. **Education completion**: Support finishing secondary education
2. **Vocational planning**: Identify interests and skills for career pathway
3. **Apprenticeship or training**: If interested, structured training in skilled trade
4. **Legitimate employment**: Support transition to age-appropriate, legal work when ready
5. **Career development**: Work is part of long-term plan, not just survival

#### Case Example: Child Labor Case (Case 5)
- **Immediate**: Removal from hazardous work; assessment; documentation
- **Short-term**: Enrollment in school; family economic assessment
- **Medium-term**: Family economic strengthening; child education support
- **Long-term**: Education completion; age-appropriate employment when legal; career planning

## Family/Reunion Domain

**Cases: 1, 2, 3, 8, and secondary in: 6, 9, 10, 11, 13, 14, 15**

### What Issues Arise in This Domain

#### Family Identification and Location
- **Unknown whereabouts**: Family location unknown; no contact information
- **Separated in chaos**: Family separated during violence, migration, or disaster
- **Lost contact**: Phone numbers disconnected; addresses no longer valid; mail not reaching
- **Identity confusion**: Child doesn't remember family names; information unclear or contradictory
- **Multiple family members**: Not knowing which family members survived or where they are
- **Trust issues**: Afraid to provide family information (fear of deportation, family arrest, etc.)

#### Family Status and Safety
- **Survival status unknown**: Not knowing if family members are alive
- **Safety assessment**: Ensuring family is not abusive or unsafe before reunification
- **Parental capacity**: Assessing whether family can provide safe care
- **Parental custody**: Legal issues (custody disputes, parental rights) complicating reunification
- **Family trauma**: Family may have undergone their own trauma; capacity to support child unknown

#### Psychological Readiness
- **Child attachment**: Degree of attachment to family; period of separation may have changed bonds
- **Child fear**: Child may fear family (if family was source of harm) or fear unknown
- **Family connection loss**: Child may have limited memory of family (separated when young)
- **Ambivalent feelings**: Child may feel conflicted about reunification
- **Caregiver transition**: Child bonded with current caregiver; fear of losing that relationship

#### Preparation and Planning
- **Staged reunification**: Gradual introduction rather than abrupt reunion
- **Communication preparation**: Photos, calls, messages before in-person meeting
- **Practical planning**: Travel logistics, timing, location of reunion
- **Family preparation**: Family members may need preparation and support too
- **Support planning**: Ongoing support after reunion to sustain family stability

#### Post-Reunification Support
- **Family adjustment**: Helping family adjust to changed child and circumstances
- **Living situation**: Ensuring adequate housing and living conditions
- **Income stability**: Family economic security to prevent separation again
- **Education continuation**: Child's education continuing after reunification
- **Health and medical care**: Accessing healthcare in family location
- **Mental health support**: Both child and family may need ongoing counseling
- **Follow-up contact**: Monitoring for stability; addressing problems early

### Relevant Laws and Conventions

**International**
- **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**
  - Article 8: Right to identity (knowing family)
  - Article 9: Right to family life (not separated except for safety)
  - Article 10: Right to family reunification
  - Article 3: Best interests of child in all decisions
- **International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)**: Right to family life
- **Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)**: Family protection and rights
- **UNHCR Guidelines**: Family tracing and reunification procedures
- **ICRC Red Crescent Movement**: Family contact and reunification services

**Regional**
- **European Convention on Human Rights**: Right to family life
- **African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child**: Family protection and reunification rights
- **Inter-American Convention on Human Rights**: Family protection

**National Examples**
- **France**: Family reunification legal process; CNDA (Court for Asylum Disputes); OFII (Office for Immigration and Integration)
- **Belgium**: Family reunification pathway; guardianship laws
- **Germany**: Spousal and family reunification provisions; legal framework
- **UK**: Family reunification visa category; eligibility requirements
- **U.S.**: Family reunification pathways; Special Immigrant Juvenile Status (SIJS); humanitarian programs

**Child Protection Standards**
- **ICRC Manual on International Humanitarian Law**: Family separation and reunification in conflict
- **Save the Children Standards**: Child protection and family reunification

### Key Resources

**Family Tracing and Reunification Services**
- **ICRC (International Committee of Red Cross)**: Central Tracing Agency; messaging; family reunification
- **UNHCR**: Family reunification programs
- **Red Crescent/Red Cross**: National chapters; family tracing services
- **International Organization for Migration (IOM)**: Family reunification assistance

**Legal Services**
- Immigration attorneys specializing in family reunification
- Public legal aid services
- NGOs providing legal assistance
- Government immigration offices
- Consulates and embassies

**Psychosocial Support**
- Family therapists and counselors

### Intervention Strategies

#### Immediate (Days 1-7)
1. **Family identification**: Gather whatever information child knows about family
   - Names (full names; nicknames; spelling variations)
   - Location (city, region, landmarks if address unknown)
   - Contact information (phone, addresses, email)
   - Other family members' names if known
2. **Safety assessment**: Is family safe to reunify with? Any concerns about family?
3. **Begin tracing**: Initiate contact through ICRC, consulates, local authorities
4. **Document information**: Record details; avoid pressure or leading questions

#### Short-term (Weeks 1-8)
1. **Active family location**: Intensive tracing through multiple channels (ICRC, consulates, Red Crescent, local authorities, networks)
2. **Family identity confirmation**: When potential match found, confirm relationship
3. **Family status assessment**: Health, safety, and capacity of family members
4. **Child preparation**: Prepare child psychologically for reunification; manage expectations
5. **Contact facilitation**: If family located, arrange safe communication (messages, photos, phone calls)
6. **Barrier identification**: Identify any obstacles to reunification (legal status, safety concerns, etc.)

#### Medium-term (Weeks 2-6 months)
1. **Staged reunification planning**:
   - Video calls if technology available
   - Recorded messages from family
   - Photos and written letters
   - Short in-person visits if possible before permanent reunion
2. **Family preparation**: Help family understand child's current status, needs, changes
3. **Child psychological preparation**: Address fears, clarify expectations, prepare for reunion
4. **Practical logistics**: Travel documents, visa if needed, timing, location of reunion
5. **Support planning**: What support family will need after reunion (housing, income, services)
6. **Health coordination**: Medical information sharing; healthcare access planning in family location

#### Long-term (Months 3-24)
1. **Reunification facilitation**: Arrange and support reunion; skilled worker present if possible
2. **Post-reunion monitoring**: Regular contact to assess adjustment
3. **Family economic support**: If needed, provide support for family stability (income assistance, housing, etc.)
4. **Education continuation**: Ensure child's schooling continues in new location
5. **Health care access**: Ensure family can access healthcare child needs
6. **Mental health support**: Provide ongoing counseling for child and family as needed
7. **Problem-solving**: Address issues arising post-reunion; modify plan as needed
8. **Sustain connection**: For children whose reunification is not possible, maintain family contact and hope

#### Case Example: Karim (Case 3)
- **Immediate**: Basic information gathering; family fear assessment
- **Short-term**: Active family tracing through ICRC and local networks
- **Medium-term**: Family identification (mother alive); video calls and messaging; child and family psychological preparation
- **Long-term**: Reunification planning; supervised return journey; ongoing family support

## Summary of Domain Interconnections

Most cases require intervention across multiple domains:

| Intersection | Common Issues | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Asylum + Health** | Medical evidence for persecution; health needs during asylum process; medication during detention | Refugee with disability; torture documentation |
| **Asylum + Family** | Family located; safe reunification in origin country; family sponsorship | Separated family reunification |
| **Health + Family** | Family economic insecurity driving health crisis; parental capacity with health condition | Child illness; parent depression |
| **Family + Economics** | Family poverty driving exploitation; economic support enabling reunification; remittances | Child labor because family poor; reunification costs |
| **Employment + Asylum** | Work authorization depends on status; work as exploitation during asylum process | Trafficked child; undocumented teenager |
| **All domains** | Complex cases with all vulnerabilities present | Mixed vulnerability and complex scenario cases |

## Using This Index by Domain

**For Individual Case Planning:**
1. Identify primary domain (where most intervention needed)
2. Identify secondary domains (other issues requiring attention)
3. Sequence interventions (immediate needs vs. longer-term planning)
4. Ensure integrated planning across domains
5. Coordinate with multiple specialists

**For Program Planning:**
- Develop domain expertise (asylum, health, employment, family reunification)
- Create protocols for domain-specific work
- Plan for cross-domain coordination and communication
- Build team with domain expertise
- Ensure staff understand connections between domains

**For System Coordination:**
- Establish agreements between agencies working in different domains
- Create referral pathways
- Share information appropriately across domains
- Coordinate planning and implementation
- Monitor outcomes across domains

**For Training and Supervision:**
- Develop domain-specific training
- Teach staff to recognize domain issues
- Develop skills in multiple domains
- Teach case coordination across domains
- Use cases to develop domain expertise
