# Obligations Légales du Tuteur / Legal Obligations - Comprehensive Guide

## Foundation: Legal Framework

### Primary Legislation
- **Code de l'action sociale et des familles (CASF)**
  - Articles L112-3, L112-4: Tutor roles and responsibilities
  - Articles L224-1 to L224-6: Child protection principles
  - Articles L211-1 to L211-6: Children's rights
  - Mandatory obligations: safety, supervision, reporting

- **Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC)**
  - International legal standard for child protection
  - Right to life, safety, education, family contact
  - Best interests of the child principle

- **French Constitution**
  - Fundamental right to education
  - Equality and non-discrimination
  - Right to family life and privacy

### Implementing Regulations
- CGRA guidelines for asylum and refugee procedures
- Rectorat (Ministry of Education) requirements for school access
- Local child protection authority procedures
- Professional standards for educational mentors

## Core Obligations

### 1. DUTY OF CARE (Devoir de Vigilance)

**Definition**: Legally binding obligation to maintain child's safety and wellbeing.

**What This Means Practically**:
- Provide appropriate supervision for child's age and needs
- Maintain safe physical environment (free from hazards)
- Monitor child's physical and emotional health
- Respond promptly to signs of distress or danger
- Maintain professional boundaries
- Document observations and actions

**Legal Standard**: Reasonable care that a competent guardian would provide.

**Consequences of Breach**:
- Civil liability: Lawsuit for damages
- Criminal liability: If negligence results in injury
- Professional consequences: Loss of guardianship rights
- Administrative consequences: Disciplinary action

**Defense**:
- Documented evidence of care provided
- Professional standards followed
- Reasonable decisions made with available information
- Prompt response to incidents

---

### 2. MANDATORY REPORTING (Devoir de Signalement)

**Who Must Report**: You are a mandatory reporter for all children under your supervision.

**What Must Be Reported**:
- **Physical abuse**: Any injury inconsistent with child's development/explanation
  - Examples: bruises in various healing stages, burns, fractures, head injuries
  - Standard: Reasonable belief, not proof required

- **Sexual abuse**: Any sexual contact or exploitation
  - Examples: Inappropriate touching, exposure to sexual content, grooming behavior
  - Standard: Any suspicion of sexual contact requires reporting

- **Emotional abuse**: Systematic harm to emotional development
  - Examples: Threats, intimidation, humiliation, isolation, controlling behavior
  - Standard: Pattern of harmful treatment

- **Neglect**: Failure to provide basic needs
  - Examples: Inadequate food, medical care, hygiene, supervision
  - Standard: Deliberate or reckless failure to meet needs

**When to Report**:
- **Immediate** (within hours): If child in imminent danger
  - Call emergency services (15: SAMU, 17: Police, 18: Pompiers)
  - Then notify child protection authority
  
- **Urgent** (within 24 hours): If abuse suspected
  - Contact local child protection authority
  - Notify your supervisor/manager
  - Document your report

**How to Report**:
- **Local Child Protection Authority** (vary by region)
  - Contact information in **05_Ressources > Contacts_Urgence.md**
  - Provide: Child's name, age, specific observations, suspected perpetrator
  
- **Police or Gendarmerie**: If crime appears to have occurred
  - Evidence may be required
  - Criminal investigation will follow
  
- **Your Supervisor**: Always notify management immediately
  - Creates paper trail
  - Ensures organizational awareness
  - Provides support to you

**Your Legal Protection**:
- You are protected from retaliation by law
- Your report is confidential (within professional limits)
- You cannot be sued for good-faith reporting
- Failure to report is a criminal offense

**What NOT to Do**:
- Do NOT interrogate the child about abuse
- Do NOT promise confidentiality to the child
- Do NOT contact the suspected abuser
- Do NOT wait for "proof"
- Do NOT delay reporting to collect more information

### 3. CONFIDENTIALITY & PRIVACY (Confidentialité)

**Principle**: Child's information is confidential and protected.

**Information Protected**:
- Personal health information (medical, mental health)
- Family background and history
- Academic performance and assessment results
- Behavioral observations and developmental assessments
- Communications with parents, schools, professionals
- Any information that could identify or harm the child

**Proper Uses**:
- Communications necessary for child's care/education
- Professional consultations with other providers (with permission)
- Required legal/administrative reporting
- Child safety assessments and referrals

**Improper Uses (Prohibited)**:
- Sharing information with unauthorized persons
- Public discussion of child's case
- Discussing in social settings (family, friends)
- Posting information online or social media
- Using child's information without permission

**Exceptions to Confidentiality**:
- Child safety concerns (mandatory reporting overrides confidentiality)
- Legal requirements/court orders
- Child or parent gives explicit permission
- Professional consultation for child's benefit

**Documenting While Maintaining Confidentiality**:
- Keep records in secure location
- Limit access to authorized personnel only
- Use neutral, objective language
- Avoid personal opinions or judgments
- Store electronic records securely (password protected)
- Destroy records per legal requirements (typically 3-7 years)

---

### 4. EDUCATIONAL SUPPORT OBLIGATIONS

**Scope**: Provide appropriate academic mentoring aligned with child's needs.

**Required Actions**:
- Assess child's current educational level
- Establish clear, measurable learning goals
- Use evidence-based teaching methods
- Document progress and adjust as needed
- Communicate with school teachers regularly
- Refer to specialists if learning disabilities suspected
- Ensure alignment with school curriculum
- Maintain appropriate academic standards

**Limitations**:
- You are not responsible for entire education (school's role)
- You cannot diagnose learning disabilities (refer to specialists)
- Your role is supplemental support, not replacement
- You must work within your competency
- Ethical obligation to be honest about limitations

**Documentation Required**:
- Learning goals (specific, measurable)
- Session notes (topics covered, time spent, progress)
- Assessment results (if testing done)
- Parent communication (feedback, reports)
- Referrals made (for further assessment/services)

---

### 5. RECORD-KEEPING OBLIGATIONS

**What Must Be Recorded**:
- Session dates, times, duration
- Topics covered and learning outcomes
- Child's progress and assessments
- Behavioral observations
- Incidents or concerns
- Parent communications
- School consultations
- Any referrals or external services
- Health or safety concerns

**How to Record** (Standards):
- Legible, dated entries
- Objective observations (not opinions)
- Specific facts and examples
- Chronological order
- Secure storage
- Limited access
- Confidentiality maintained

**Retention Period**:
- Minimum 3 years after tutoring ends
- Longer if legal/medical issues involved
- Permanent if child protection concerns documented
- Follow any specific organizational requirements

**Access & Ownership**:
- Child/parent has right to access records (usually)
- Records belong to guardianship arrangement (not you)
- Surrender all records if tutoring ends
- Cooperate with legal proceedings/audits

---

### 6. PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS OBLIGATIONS

**Training & Competence**:
- Maintain current knowledge of your field
- Continue professional development
- Only work within your competency
- Be honest about your qualifications
- Seek supervision when uncertain

**Appropriate Conduct**:
- Maintain professional boundaries with child and family
- Avoid dual relationships (friend + tutor is problematic)
- Professional appearance and communication
- No use of child for personal benefit
- No exploitation of child's trust

**Ethical Decision-Making**:
- Child's best interests primary consideration
- Honest assessment of ability to help
- Transparency with parents/supervisor
- Appropriate referral to other professionals
- Willingness to walk away if not helping

---

### 7. MANDATORY REPORTING INCIDENTS (NOT Abuse)

**Reportable Non-Abuse Concerns**:
- Child not attending school (truancy)
- Inability to provide basic needs (parent financial crisis)
- Child expressing suicidal ideation
- Substance abuse indicators
- Gang involvement or exploitation
- Human trafficking indicators
- Extreme parental mental health crisis

**Reporting Process**:
- Consult with supervisor first
- Make written report to appropriate authority
- Document your report
- Follow up on outcomes
- Support child as appropriate

---

### 8. FINANCIAL & INSURANCE OBLIGATIONS

**Tax Compliance**:
- Register as self-employed or micro-entrepreneur (if independent)
- Maintain invoices and payment records
- File annual tax returns
- Pay social contributions as required
- Keep financial records for minimum 6 years

**Insurance Requirements**:
- Professional liability insurance (strongly recommended, may be required)
- Coverage includes: negligence, confidentiality breach, bodily injury
- Minimum coverage varies by jurisdiction
- Maintain proof of coverage
- Notify insurer of any incidents

**Financial Record-Keeping**:
- Hours worked (date, time, duration)
- Rate agreed and amount paid
- Invoices issued
- Receipts for expenses (if applicable)
- Bank statements matching payments

## Situation-Specific Obligations

### When Working with Child in Care System
- Know the child's case plan and goals
- Coordinate with case workers
- Report progress to social services as required
- Understand any legal restrictions or placement terms
- Maintain confidentiality regarding case details

### When Working with Asylum-Seeking/Refugee Minors
- Understand legal status and related rights
- Support asylum procedure participation
- Maintain appropriate boundaries regarding family contact
- Know reporting requirements (CGRA, immigration authorities)
- Understand access to services (healthcare, education, housing)

### When Working with Special Needs Children
- Understand the child's disabilities/conditions
- Implement accommodations appropriately
- Coordinate with medical/therapeutic providers
- Maintain appropriate expectations
- Document progress relative to individual needs

### When Working with Trauma-Affected Children
- Understand trauma responses and triggers
- Practice trauma-informed care
- Know crisis response procedures
- Connect with appropriate mental health services
- Maintain safe, predictable environment

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

### Civil Consequences
- Lawsuit for damages (injuries, emotional harm)
- Loss of guardianship/tutoring rights
- Liability for medical/therapeutic costs
- Compensation to child/family

### Criminal Consequences
- Prosecution for child endangerment
- Failure to report abuse (criminal offense in most jurisdictions)
- Criminal negligence charges
- Imprisonment possible in serious cases

### Administrative Consequences
- Suspension or termination of guardianship
- Professional license revocation (if applicable)
- Mandatory retraining before reinstatement
- Background check flagging
- Employment barriers

### Professional Consequences
- Reputation damage
- Loss of professional references
- Difficulty obtaining future clients
- Exclusion from professional organizations
- Public disclosure of violations

---

## Key Legal Principles

### Best Interests of the Child
- Primary consideration in all decisions
- When uncertain, choose option protecting child
- May require sacrifice of your convenience
- Overrides other considerations in safety matters

### Duty Not to Harm
- Do no intentional or foreseeable harm
- Protect from dangers you're aware of
- Anticipate risks appropriate to age
- Act on professional judgment

### Right to Protection
- Child has fundamental right to safety
- Your role is to implement this right
- No exceptions for parental rights
- Supersedes privacy, confidentiality in safety cases

### Good Faith Compliance
- Reasonable attempts to follow obligations
- Documentation proves compliance
- Decisions based on available information
- Professional standards as guide
